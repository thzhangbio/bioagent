SUMMARY

Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) is strongly resistant to immunotherapy. However, recent evidence shows that some PDAC tumors contain tertiary lymphoid structures (TLSs) associated with improved survival. Why TLS forms in some tumors but not others remains elusive. Using a lymphotoxin beta receptor (LTBR) agonist, we observe the induction of TLS-aggregates in some murine PDAC tumor models but not others. The phenotypes of cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs) in TLS-resistant models are myofibroblastic (myCAF), whereas TLS-permissive models are enriched with reticular-CAF (rCAF) subsets. Differentiation into myCAF blocks the LTBR-mediated upregulation of chemokines and lymphocyte migration toward fibroblasts. Inhibiting the transforming growth factor β (TGFβ) receptor, combined with LTBR agonism, promotes TLS formation and T cell-dependent tumor control. In patient tumors, rCAF are proximal to TLS, while myCAF are distally located. These data indicate that myCAF represses rCAF programming critical for TLS formation but can be therapeutically remodeled to promote immune control of PDAC tumors.

INTRODUCTION

Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) is an aggressive and highly lethal malignancy due to the lack of early detection and therapeutic insensitivity.1 Nearly half of patients with PDAC are diagnosed with metastatic disease, while also having a less than 5% objective response rate to current immunotherapies.2–4 Thus, there is an urgent need to develop improved treatments and novel biomarkers that will improve survival. Despite resistance to immunotherapies, we and others have reported significant lymphocyte infiltration in tumors from a subset of patients with PDAC that is equivalent or even superior to other cancer types, many of which do respond to immunotherapy.5–7 Furthermore, these lymphocyte-inflamed signatures associate with longer overall survival.8 Hence, despite the belief that pancreatic cancer is a poorly immunogenic tumor type, many patients with PDAC mount nascent immune responses that conceivably can be harnessed to control tumors. One unique lymphocyte-inflamed subtype, those with tertiary lymphoid structures (TLSs), is prognostic for survival in all solid tumor types, including PDAC, and predictive of response to immune checkpoint blockade in melanoma and sarcoma.9–13 TLS are a lymph node-like aggregation comprised of T cells, B cells, and antigen-presenting cells observed in chronically inflamed peripheral tissues, such as solid tumors.13 Our prior study of treatmentnaı¨ve patients with PDAC showed that 44% of surgically resected tumors displayed histological presence of TLS associated with improved survival and downregulated transforming growth factor β(TGFβ) signaling.7 In other mouse tumor models, the depletion or suppression of Tregs, a major source of TGFβ in the tumor microenvironment, elicits TLS formation.14–16 This indicates that, in addition to expressing the requisite pro-inflammatory mediators of TLS formation, some tumors lack or have overcome local immunosuppression to form TLS.

Recently, various groups have described cancer-associated fibroblast (CAF) subsets critical for promoting adaptive immunity in tumors. Reflecting the fibroblastic reticular cell with lymphoid tissue organizing (LTO) properties in lymph nodes, parallel functions have been described for TLS-organizing reticular CAF (rCAF) subsets.17–22 Signaling from tumor necrosis factor receptor (TNFR) and the lymphotoxin beta receptor (LTBR) converge to activate these TLS-promoting CAF phenotypes in complimentary ways.23 While TNFR1/2 is required for the initial aggregation of the reticular network, LTBR expands and organizes the TLS.24 These pathways are chiefly responsible for the upregulation of CCL19, CCL21, and CXCL13 in CAF, while other groups have also reported on the pro-inflammatory effects of type 1 and type 2 interferons in stromal-mediated T cell recruitment.25–27 However, most patients with cancer do not present with TLS in their tumors, and these CAF phenotypes are rare in PDAC, where the trademark desmoplastic stroma is governed predominantly by TGFβ-programmed myofibroblasts (my-CAF).28–31 Therefore, while we are beginning to understand the positive signals that reinforce TLS formation, little is understood about the negative regulators that restrict this biology and how to overcome them therapeutically. In this study, we investigated the mechanisms of how CAF phenotypic programming regulates TLS formation. These data indicate that in tumor microenvironments without TLS, immunosuppressive stromal elements counteract pro-inflammatory mediators by abrogating reticular CAF differentiation.

RESULTS

Mouse KPC cell lines display tumor heterogeneity and respond to LTBR agonism

To understand the heterogeneity of the stromal immune response and its relationship to PDAC progression, we characterized the phenotype of nine PDAC cell lines derived from KPC(32) (Pdxℐ –Cre/LSL –KRas(Gℐ2D/+)/LSL –p53(Rℐ72H/+)) transgenic mouse tumors (KPCL-1-4/KPC-5-9).33,34 KPCL-1-4 lines include a fourth transgene encoding a luciferase protein fused to the model H2-Kb restricted antigen, SIYRYYGL, for tracking of tumor-reactive CD8(+) T cells (Figures S1A and S1B ).35–37 KPC cells were implanted orthotopically into C57BL/6 mice, and tumor growth kinetics were monitored until tumors reached 100 mm(2) or greater. These cell lines expressed low basal levels of MHC-I and PD-L1, but treatment with type I (IFNβ) and type II (IFNγ) interferon upregulated surface MHC-I and PD-L1, indicating their responsiveness to T cell-mediated inflammation (Figures S1C and S1D). Despite their common genetic origin, we observed substantial differences in orthotopic tumor growth kinetics (Figure 1A). To assess the capacity for TLS formation in vivo, we tested the response of these tumors to a LTBR monoclonal antibody with agonist activity (αLTBR). LTBR is a necessary gene for lymph node organogenesis,38 and various groups have shown LTBR agonism can elicit TLS in mouse tumor models.24,39–42 Both KPCL-1 and KPC-6 orthotopic tumors displayed a significant reduction in tumor growth following αLTBR treatment compared to isotype control groups (Figure 1B). Histological evaluation of tumor sections from αLTBR-treated mice showed evidence of lymphoid aggregates on tumor margins with PNAd+ high endothelial venules (HEVs) and CXCL13(+) cells present within the aggregates, whereas this was rarely observed in isotype-treated controls (Figure 1C). Immunofluorescence revealed that early TLS-aggregates (E-TLS) in αLTBR-treated tumors are comprised primarily of B cells with CD3(+)CD8(−) T cells and CD8(+) T cells surrounding the aggregate (Figures 1D and 1E). In line with the detection of HEV and lymphocyte recruit- ment in these tumors, the density of CXCL13+ cells and T cells was increased in αLTBR-treated KPC-6 tumors (Figure 1F). In all KPC tumors, surface LTBR expression was present on numerous cell types, including myeloid cells, tumor cells, endothelial cells, and fibroblasts (Figure 1G). However, stromal fibroblasts and endothelial cells consistently expressed the highest levels of surface LTBR across all KPC tumor models at multiple timepoints tested (Figure 1G). Podoplanin-expressing (PDPN+ ) CAF were numerically higher than CD31+ endothelial cells, suggesting that CAF are a primary target of LTBR ligands in KPC tumors (Figure 1H). Importantly, even though KPC tumor cells maintained robust expression of LTBR, they did not upregulate PD-L1 or MHC-I in response to αLTBR treatment (Figures S1C–S1E), indicating that the pro-inflammatory response in vivo is tumor cell extrinsic. Cytokine and chemokine profiling of KPC/KPCL cell lines in vitro revealed significant heterogeneity in the baseline secretory phenotype, primarily myeloid cell factors such as GM-CSF, IL-6, CCL2, CXCL1, CXCL2, and CXCL5 (Figure S1F). These data demonstrate that agonizing LTBR is a reproducible method to induce lymphocyte recruitment and E-TLS formation, allowing us to assess the mechanisms that influence TLS development and function in heterogeneous PDAC models.

CAF phenotypes associate with TLS permissiveness in PDAC tumors

We then investigated the response of other KPC cell lines to αLTBR treatment. While the KPC-6 and KPCL-1 lines responded to treatment with αLTBR, the KPCL-4 and KPCL-3 lines did not generate lymphoid aggregates, nor did they exhibit a growth delay (Figure 2A). For this reason, the KPC-6 and KPCL-1 lines were termed ‘‘TLS-permissive’’ and the KPCL-4 and KPCL-3 lines were termed ‘‘TLS-resistant’’ allowing us to study the features of tumors receptive to TLS formation in vivo. Several groups have shown that specific CAF phenotypes can regulate the lymphocyte recruitment and organization of the reticular network in other mouse tumor models. TLS-permissive tumors were enriched for ICAM-1+ VCAM-1+ rCAFs, whereas TLS-resistant tumors had a predominant ICAM-1+ VCAM-1- myCAF phenotype (Figures 2B and S2A). TGFβ antagonizes the TNFα/LTBR induction of reticular programming in fibroblasts

Previous studies show TNFR1/2 and LTBR signaling in CAF cooperate to promote LTO functions in tumors.24 To understand how these signaling pathways regulate fibroblast differentiation in this manner, we isolated primary dermal fibroblasts from mice and exposed them to various treatments in vitro. Fibroblasts were treated with TNFα, αLTBR, or in combination and analyzed by flow cytometry for differentiation markers. TNFα and αLTBR treatment alone upregulated expression of VCAM-1, which was enhanced by the co-treatment of these ligands (Figure 3Ai). Expression of PDGFRα was also upregulated in treated fibroblasts with a concomitant downregulation of PDGFRβ (Figures 3Aii-iii) while TNFα treatment alone slightly reduced LTBR expression on fibroblasts (Figure 3Aiv). These observations and the variable response to αLTBR agonism in vivo (Figures 2A–2C) prompted us to identify negative regulators of TNFR/LTBR signaling in fibroblasts. PDGFRβ has been implicated as a direct target of TGFβ signaling,48 and a marker for TGFβ -induced myCAF differentiation.49 Furthermore, we previously showed patients with TLS(+) PDAC have downregulated TGFβ signaling and myCAF markers such as LRRC15.7,28 To that end, we hypothesized that the TGFβ induction of myCAF programming modulates the response to LTBR agonism. In contrast to αLTBR treatment, TGFβ1 suppressed VCAM-1, PDGFRα, and LTBR expression but upregulated PDGFRβ expression (Figures 3Bi-iv). Moreover, TGFβ1 pre-treatment of fibroblasts prevented αLTBR-mediated increases in VCAM-1 and PDGFRα cell surface expression (Figures 3Bi-ii). Co-culture of naive fibroblasts with tumor-conditioned media from TLSpermissive and resistant cell lines did not reproduce these effects, indicating paracrine tumor cell-fibroblast crosstalk is not responsible for CAF differentiation in these contexts (Figure S3A). Upregulation and secretion of the lymphocyte chemoattractants, Cxcl13 and Ccl19, required co-treatment with both TNFα and αLTBR (Figures 3Ci-ii and 4A), and again, this was abolished by pre-treatment with TGFβ1 at the transcriptional (Figures 3Ci-ii) and protein level (Figures 4B and 4C). Correspondingly, fibroblasts upregulated canonical myCAF markers Acta2 and Ctgf in TGFβ1-treated groups (Figures 3Ciii-iv). Other T cell chemoattractants, such as Cxcl9 and Ccl21a, were not significantly induced by TNFα/αLTBR treatment (Figures S3Biii), but numerous other chemokines were and could be inhibited by TGFβ1 pre-treatment (Figure S3C). Co-treatment of fibroblasts with TNFα and soluble LIGHT resulted in a 3-fold increase in Cxcl13 mRNA expression, but this was markedly less than with TNFα/αLTBR (Figure S3D), indicating that the LTBR agonist antibody delivers a more potent signal than endogenous LTBR ligands. The induction of the iCAF phenotype by IL-1α treatment or exposure to interferons alone or in combination with αLTBR did not induce rCAF differentiation or chemokine expression (Figures S3Ei-iii). As tissue-specific differences in fibroblast phenotype have been demonstrated, fibroblasts from normal mouse pancreas were isolated and compared to dermal fibroblasts for baseline marker expression and responsiveness to TGFβ1 and TNFα/αLTBR. Flow cytometry revealed that both dermal and pancreatic fibroblasts had similar expression profiles, including LTBR. Moreover, treatment with TGFβ1 and TNFα/αLTBR modulated expression of VCAM-1, PDGFRα, PDGFRβ, and LTBR similarly to dermal fibroblasts (Figures S3F and S3G). Further analysis of in vitro activated dermal fibroblasts showed global changes in CD105, CD34, Ly6A, CD74, CD21/ CD35, TNFR, and HVEM between rCAF and myCAF differentiation conditions (Figure S3H). Taken together, these data show that TNFR/LTBR-mediated reticular cell programming in fibroblasts is abrogated by the TGFβ -induction of the myCAF phenotype.

Lymphocyte migration to reticular fibroblasts is suppressed by TGFβ

The differentiation of rCAF phenotypes by TNFR/LTBR and lack of CXCL13 secretion from our KPC cancer cell lines (Figure 4A) suggest lymphocyte recruitment to these tumors is primarily regulated by CAF, similar to the functions of fibroblastic reticular cells in lymph nodes.50 In support of this, the TNFα/αLTBR treatment of cultured fibroblasts resulted in the secretion of CXCL13 and CCL19 that was abrogated by TGFβ1 pre-conditioning (Figures 4B and 4C). We next conducted migration assays by culturing naive fibroblasts in the bottom well, followed by the addition of purified B or T cells (Figures S4A–S4E) to the upper transwell (Figure 4D). We first assessed naive B cells for their migratory potential to activated fibroblasts (Figure S4A). Migration of naive B cells to TNFα/αLTBR-activated fibroblasts was significantly increased compared to untreated fibroblasts (Figure 4E) but TGFβ1 pre-treatment suppressed this migration (Figure 4E). Naive CD8(+) T cells and T(H)1 (Tbet+ IFNγ+ ) polarized CD4(+) T cells (Figures S4A and S4B) also displayed enhanced migration to TNFR/LTBR-activated fibroblasts, and this migration was again repressed when fibroblasts were differentiated into myCAF (Figures 4F and 4G). Both T and B cell subsets expressed varying levels of the cognate CXCL13 and CCL19 receptors, CXCR5 and CCR7 (Figures S4C–S4E), but these expression patterns changed upon the BCR-stimulation of B cells (Figure S4E). Despite this, activated B cells migrated similarly to naive B cells, albeit at reduced migration numbers (Figure S4F). Importantly, the treatment of fibroblasts alone with TGFβR1i did not upregulate CxcI13 or Ccl19, indicating a requirement for TNFR and LTBR signaling engagement to induce chemokine expression (Figures 5A and 5B). Correspondingly, inhibiting TGFβR1 signaling in TNFα/αLTBR-stimulated fibroblasts alleviated the TGFβ -mediated suppression of B cell migration (Figure 5C). Reprogramming myCAF to rCAF increases T and B cell infiltration and anti-tumor immune phenotype

We next investigated the immune response associated with rCAF reprogramming. Supporting the increased rCAF differentiation in tumors of αLTBR/TGFβR1i-treated and CAF-Alk5 KO mice (Figures 5F, 5J, and S5L–S5N), we observed significant upregulation of lymphocyte chemokines in CAF sorted from tumors (Figure 6A). While LTBR agonism alone induced the expression of Ccl19, Ccl21a, and Cxcl13 from CAF, blocking TGFβR1 had the most significant impact on Γ(CI19) and CxcI13 upregulation (Figure 6B). Furthermore, TGFβR1i reduced myCAF markers Acta2 and Ctgf, while αLTBR and TGFβR1i further suppressed myCAF gene expression in treated tumors (Figure S6Ai-ii). Correspondingly, tumors from αLTBR/TGFβR1i treated animals demonstrated an increase in total B cell, CD4(+) T cell, and CD8(+) T cell infiltration (Figures 6C and 6D). Increases in immune infiltration associated with the formation of E-TLS in αLTBR/ TGFβR1i treated tumors (Figure 6E). LTBR agonism alone increased E-TLS density and number (Figures 6F and 6G), suggesting that αLTBR can induce local immune aggregation while TGFβR1i is required to enhance intratumoral T/B cell trafficking and increase tumor control. When combined with chemotherapy, we observed that LTBR agonism largely drove CD4(+) T cell infiltration while TGFβR1 inhibition increased CD8(+) T cell infiltration into tumors (Figures S6B and S6C). There were no significant differences in the frequency of total tumor-reactive CD8(+) T cells (Figures S6D and S6G), but SIY-reactive stem-like phenotypes did increase following combination treatment (Figures S6E and S6F). Despite the critical role of TNFα in driving chemokine expression in CAF (Figure 4A), we did not detect an increase in TNFα+ cells after treatment (Figure S6H). We next asked if CAF chemokine upregulation (Figure 6B) was the primary mechanism of increased T and B cell infiltration in vivo. Neutralization of CXCL13 ameliorated the increase in B cell but not T cell tumor infiltration in treated groups, confirming that TGFβ and LTBR signaling in CAF are the primary regulators of this B cell chemotaxis pathway (Figure S6I). However, CXCL13 blockade had no impact on αLTBR/TGFβR1i treatment efficacy (Figure 6H), and B cell depletion with anti-CD20 in TLS-permissive KPC-6 tumors did not reverse αLTBR treatment efficacy, indicating that intratumoral B cells are not required for tumor control in this context (Figures S6J and S6K). We then hypothesized that enhanced T cell activity following therapy was facilitating these treatment responses. Indeed, the depletion of CD4(+) and CD8(+) T cells reversed the efficacy of αLTBR/TGFβR1i treatment, proving that the therapeutic efficacy was dependent on T cells (Figures 6I–6K). Interestingly, despite a similar number of total E-TLS, T cell depletion decreased aggregate size and abolished CD8+ T cell residency within E-TLS (Figures 6L and 6M), indicating that B cell aggregates alone are not functional and require T cells to mediate anti-tumor immunity. These data show that CAF phenotype can be therapeutically modulated to enhance T and B cell infiltration in PDAC tumors to overcome the TLS-resistant tumor microenvironment and improve anti-tumor immunity.

rCAF are spatially associated with TLS in human patients with PDAC

To translate these preclinical findings to patients with PDAC, we assessed the spatial relationships between CAF phenotype and TLS formation in human PDAC tumors using spatial transcriptomics. We first created a TLS-aggregate signature from spatially deconvolved transcriptomic data and validated this from pathologist annotations of H&E sections and multispectral immunofluorescence for TLS-markers (Figure S7A). The Visium-defined TLS features showed good agreement with the immunofluorescent identification of B cells in TLS-regions (Figure S7B). Using a publicly available single-cell RNAsequencing dataset53 we deconvoluted CAF phenotypes from 6 transcriptionally and spatially distinct CAF subsets (Figure S7C) and determined their proximity to TLS adjacent regions (Figure 7A). Indeed, rCAFs expressing CCL19 and CCL21 were significantly enriched in the proximal regions encapsulating TLS, while myCAFs were more frequently distributed distally to TLS (Figures 7B and S7D). This corresponded to a significantly lower distance of rCAF to TLS regions and a greater distance of myCAF to TLS regions (Figure 7C). Furthermore, we identified TLS proximal rCAF subsets had higher activation of TNFR and non-canonical NF-κB signaling and reduced TGFβR signaling relative to my-CAF (Figures 7D, 7E, and S7E), supporting the opposing roles of TNFR/LTBR signaling and TGFβR signaling in these CAF subsets. Taken together, these data strongly indicate that NFκB-activated rCAF are necessary for TLS formation and T cell-dependent tumor immunity, while TGFβR-differentiated myCAF opposes rCAF differentiation and TLS neogenesis in mouse and human PDAC tumors.

However, in human tumors, other studies have implicated CXCL13-expressing T cells as functional contributors to TLS maintenance69,70 indicating there are important species-specific differences.

Despite a common genetic origin of our KPC cell models, these tumors exhibited a nascent transcriptional and cellular heterogeneity reflective of patients with PDAC (Figures 1 and S1). This corresponded to a variable response to LTBRinduced E-TLS formation that was strongly associated with the baseline CAF phenotype (Figure 2). Relieving myCAF tran- scriptional programming by TGFβR1 inhibition or CAF-conditional TGFβR1 deletion in TLS-resistant tumors allowed LTBR agonism to upregulate critical rCAF genes and enhance T cell-dependent tumor control (Figure 6). This supports other studies that repolarize myCAF with TGFβ -blocking agents, thereby alleviating the suppression of interferon-transcriptional signatures.71,72 Our study is distinct in that by concomitantly providing an LTBR agonist during myCAF blockade, CAFs are skewed to actively support T and B cell recruitment, independent of interferon, in tumor microenvironments with high TGFβ signaling. Interestingly, while LTBR agonism promoted CAF reprogramming and lymphocyte recruitment in CAF-conditional TGFβR1 knockout mice, tumor control was not increased compared to CAF-TGFβR1 KO alone. We interpret these findings that in order to achieve the full effect of TGFβ inhibition, TGFβR signaling in T cells must also be interrupted, as has been previously shown.67,73–76 Agents that agonize LTBR in the tumor have shown similar effects in other cold tumor models and lymphotoxin signaling is required for lymph node organogenesis,38,77,78 underscoring the need to develop lymphotoxin agonists as cancer therapies.47,79,80 Lymphotoxin signaling may be potentiated by STING and type 1 interferon pathway engagement.81 These strategies may circumvent the need to stimulate endogenous lymphotoxin signaling for TLS formation, as can be provided when boosting group 2 innate lymphoid cells (ILC2) activation in other PDAC models.82 Besides ILC subsets, lymphotoxin alpha and beta are commonly expressed by T cells, NK cells, and B cells in various forms.78,83–85 Because of this, one strategy to form and sustain TLS neogenesis may be by promoting T cell activity via blockade of T cell inhibitory receptors such as PD-1 and CTLA4. This may explain why immune checkpoint blockade is more efficacious in patients with TLS.9–12,86 Intriguingly, stromal TGFβ signatures and the myCAF phenotype are predictive of poorly immunogenic tumors and resistance to immune checkpoint inhibitors.28,29,57,87,88 Indeed, TGFβ signaling is one of the strongest predictors of immunotherapy failure across all cancer types89,90 indicating there are conserved mechanisms of CAF to immune cell crosstalk across cancers. Future research will help elucidate whether LTBR agonists can promote ICB response in our TLS(+) PDAC models. In tumors, rCAF phenotypes are distinct in gene expression and function from iCAF and apCAF.31,64,91 Specifically, IFNγ synergizes with TNFα to promote the expression of CXCR3 ligands from CAF in mouse PDAC tumors.25 In immunogenic B16 melanoma models, TNFR and LTBR activation of CAF were necessary for B cell recruitment, and TLS formation when implanted in the intraperitoneal cavity.24 Metastatic lesions in patients with colorectal cancer and mouse models contained a population of CCL19-expressing CAF that augmented TLS formation22 while CCL19/CCL21-expressing rCAF could be detected in tumors from patients with breast and lung cancer located directly next to TLS.66,92 The close proximity of rCAF phenotypes to TLS in human PDAC tumors (Figure 7) is in agreement with these other recent reports.92–94 Our study confirms that not only is LTBR agonism coupled with TNFR1/2 activation required for rCAF differentiation and function, but imprinting of the myCAF state by TGFβ silences this transcriptional program. Given that the majority of patient tumors are devoid of TLS(13) and myCAF are the dominant CAF phenotype across cancer types associated with immunosuppression,95 these data help to mechanistically explain these observations. As TGFβ blockade has yielded poor efficacy in patients with metastatic PDAC,96–98 we offer an alternative therapeutic strategy with associated histological and molecular biomarkers of response suitable for clinical translation. Limitations of the study

Our report possesses some limitations. Despite the treatment response and TLS-like aggregate formation to LTBR agonists and TGFβR1 inhibitors in our mouse models, we only observed early-TLS aggregate formation, devoid of germinal centers. We attribute this to the lack of strong T and B cell antigens expressed in our tumor cell lines. Indeed, the expression of hen egg lysozyme in murine lung cancer lines can induce spontaneous TLS formation.99 Second, we did not observe a functional anti-tumor role for B cells in our model. This is in contrast to other reports in mice and patients, and again is potentially ascribed to the lack of high-affinity B-cell antigens expressed in our KPC lines. Finally, the LTBR agonist likely binds endothelial cells directly to induce transdifferentiation into an HEV phenotype, but the investigation into how HEV contributed to TLS formation was beyond the scope of this study and also reported elsewhere.20,100 Future studies will be required to dissect the role of humoral responses in TLS-enhanced anti-tumor immunity and how HEV may contribute to this immunobiology.

Single cell RNA-Seq analysis

To build a scRNA-Seq human PDAC reference for spatial deconvolution, an open sourced PDAC atlas was utilized (https://zenodo. org/records/14199536) and refined using Seurat(v5) in R. For processing, post-treatment and metastatic samples were removed as well as any non-tumor samples. Cell type annotations included with the dataset were used as is, apart from fibroblasts and T cells,

which underwent subsequent refinement. Standard Seurat clustering was performed on T cells using the first 50 dimensions of the pre-existing harmony dimensions and a Louvain clustering resolution of 0.6, and manually annotated using canonical genes for T cell subsets. Fibroblast populations were manually annotated from the original authors’ clusters to more descriptive annotations. Finally, cell clusters with potential spatial ambiguity (e.g. cycling tumor vs. tumor) were removed. The single cell dataset was down-sampled to a maximum of 1000 randomly selected cells per annotation and 5000 randomly selected tumor cells, and raw counts were exported as an anndata object on .h5ad using the sceasy and reticulate R packages. Differential expression was performed using the MAST testing method and results were plotted using the EnhancedVolcano R package. Gene set enrichment analysis Gene set testing was performed for both spatial and single cell transcriptomics using the fastGSEA R package and msigdb for gene sets. Reactome and Hallmark gene set sources were used and refined to just the sets containing ‘‘TNF’’ or ‘‘TGF’’ in their name. Log2FC values were used as the scoring metric and gsea was run using the standard fgsea function, which performs their permutation based gsea method. Visium TLS identification and analysis

To identify probable TLS aggregates in Visium samples, spatial deconvolution was performed using the aforementioned single cell dataset as reference. For each Visium sample, deconvolution was performed using TACCO python package, raw Visium read counts, and the single cell reference set. Deconvolution results were exported as barcode cell proportion counts in csv format and incorporated into their respective Seurat objects. To identify spot aggregates of probable TLS, the spot proportions for all lymphocytes were summed to a total lymphoid proportion. Spots were then selected based on a lymphoid proportion of 0.5 or greater and further refined to only spots above the threshold and in groups of 3 direct neighbors also above the threshold, to be consistent with pathologist-defined TLS size constraints. TLS-adjacent spots were then isolated from each of these aggregates by selecting all spots within a 300-micron border around each aggregate. For background distributions, 7 spot clusters within tissue were randomly selected, and filtered for those non-overlapping with the TLS and TLS-adjacent annotated areas. Random selections were limited to a number equivalent to 1/100 of the available non-annotated spots, but no sample had less than 25 sampled 7-spot clusters. Quantities of cell types in these regions were averaged for all spots of the same annotation within each sample individually. For analyses involving individual spot aggregates, TLS aggregates were individually clustered using the default iGraph package graph clustering on unweighted adjacency matrices of the TLS aggregate annotated spots. CAF-TLS association analysis

To identify the spatial relationship between TLS and CAF subtypes, two approaches were used. First, Visium spots were annotated in each sample as rCAF or myCAF enriched if they had greater than 20% abundance in the spot. To capture distance between TLS aggregates and CAF species, rCAF+ and myCAF+ spots were coupled to their nearest TLS aggregate centroid. Then a Euclidean distance between rCAF+ or myCAF+ spots and their nearest TLS-aggregate was captured. The distance for rCAF and myCAF to their nearest aggregate was summarized as an average for that TLS aggregate cluster. Distances between TLS aggregate clusters and CAF species, for every sample, were aggregated altogether for analysis, but showed no apparent preference for any specific sample. The second method includes radial abundance for each TLS aggregate cluster. Briefly, each for TLS aggregate cluster in each sample, spot distances and rCAF/myCAF abundances were collected for all spots within 2mm of the cluster centroid. Spots which were previously annotated as TLS were excluded, due to relative exclusion of all non-lymphoid species. CAF abundances were modeled as a function of distance for all TLS aggregate clusters, in all samples, with generalized additive models (GAM), using the mgcv R package, which was optimal due to the large quantity of spots. Each CAF type was smoothed with a 95% confidence interval to illuminate the relationship between distance and abundance, for each type. Significance testing on the GAM smoothed curves between rCAF and myCAF was performed using approximate F-tests on the spline basis.
