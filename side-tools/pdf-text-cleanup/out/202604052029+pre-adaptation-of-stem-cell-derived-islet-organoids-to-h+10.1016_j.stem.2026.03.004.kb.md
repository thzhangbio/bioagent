# INTRODUCTION

Diabetes mellitus, a global health crisis affecting 589 million people worldwide,1 is characterized by the loss or dysfunction of pancreatic β cells, leading to impaired glucose homeostasis. The generation and transplantation of human pluripotential stem cell-derived islet organoids (SC-islets) represent a crucial advancement in stem cell research, with the goal of establishing a promising approach for diabetes treatment.2–9 However, the clinical application of SC-islets is hindered by three major challenges, i.e., functional immaturity, susceptibility to hypoxia stress, and lack of vascularization post-transplantation, ultimately compromising therapeutic efficacy. Angiogenesis is a critical determinant of islet transplantation success. Unlike these hypoxia-resilient cells, β cells exhibit extreme metabolic fragility; their reliance on mitochondrial respiration for glucose-stimulated insulin secretion results in rapid ${ \sf O } _ { 2 }$ depletion, rendering them uniquely susceptible to hypoxic damage, accelerated apoptosis, and functional impairment under compromised ${ \sf O } _ { 2 }$ supply.26–30 While the hypoxic environment of transplantation sites can promote angiogenesis, early-stage hypoxia exerts detrimental effects on SC-islets, including oxidative stress, apoptosis, and impaired function.10,12,31,32 This paradoxical effect limits the expression of VEGFA, thereby creating a barrier to effective graft integration. Therefore, it is critical to develop a hypoxia-tolerant approach to induce VEGFA expression, enabling rapid angiogenesis at the beginning of SC-islet transplantation. However, how to resolve this discrepancy remains elusive. AMPK, a central hub in cellular energy homeostasis, is critically dysregulated in type 2 diabetes (T2D), where its diminished activity exacerbates pancreatic islet dysfunction and metabolic stress.33,34 Restoring AMPK signaling has emerged as a therapeutic strategy, as its activation enhances glucose utilization, reverses insulin resistance, and promotes β cell maturation while conferring resilience against oxidative and endoplasmic reticulum stress.33,35–37 Notably, AMPK orchestrates hypoxia adaptation and vascular remodeling by upregulating VEGFA expression, enhancing endothelial cell functionality and reprogramming metabolic responses to ${ \sf O } _ { 2 }$ deprivation.38–40 Intriguingly, zinc is a trace element essential for insulin crystallization in β cells.41,42 While the basal level of zinc is indispensable for cell function and survival, its excessive accumulation in SC-islets inhibits insulin secretion43 and, in neurons, disrupts AMPK activity.44,45 This zinc-AMPK interplay is further highlighted by studies demonstrating that zinc chelation rescues neuronal survival by suppressing zinc-mediated excitotoxicity and restoring AMPK phosphorylation, indicating a translatable approach to mitigate zinc overload in SC-islets.46,47 Building on this paradigm, we propose that pharmacological inhibition of zinc transportation in SC-islets may reactivate AMPK signaling, thereby simultaneously addressing three critical barriers to transplantation success; these include enhancing functional maturation by alleviating zinc-induced secretory inhibition, bolstering hypoxia resistance through AMPK-driven metabolic adaptation, and accelerating graft vascularization. Here, this study addresses three critical challenges, i.e., immaturity, ischemia-induced failure, and lack of vascularization of SC-islets post-transplantation. Specifically, with singlecell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) and single-nucleus assay for transposase-accessible chromatin sequencing (snATACseq), we show that zinc accumulation in SC-islet β cells inhibits AMPK activity, and its chemical inhibition activates AMPK, downregulates HIF1A, and upregulates HIF1Aindependent VEGFA expression—thereby promoting endothelial cell integration and angiogenesis. Importantly, in the diabetic animal model, this approach improves hypoxia resistance, accelerates graft angiogenesis, and enhances glycemic control.

# RESULTS

# Excessive zinc induces elevated ROS and consequent oxidation of AMPK in SC-islets

Zinc, an essential trace element, plays a pivotal role in insulin crystallization, storage, and secretion in pancreatic β cells.41,42,48 By contrast, excessive zinc disrupts AMPK activity in neural cells.44,45 Based on these, we hypothesized that zinc overload in SC-islets may induce AMPK inactivation and hamper SC-islet function. To test this, we generated SC-islets using three independent human embryonic stem cell (hESC) lines, Mel1-reporter (Mel1-R, INSGFP/W-NKX6.1mCherry/mCherry, labeling SC-islet β cells), HuES8, and ZB13AD-B (AD-B), through a seven-stage differentiation protocol. This process sequentially yielded definitive endoderm, pancreatic progenitor, endocrine progenitor, and SC-islets with a cellular composition comparable to human primary islets (Figures 1A–1E). During differentiation, zinc accumulation progressively intensified (Figures 1F, 1G, and S1A), peaking in SC-islets. Strikingly, these SC-islets exhibited pronounced β cell heterogeneity characterized by spatially distinct zincand insulin-enriched subpopulations (Figures 1H and 1I), revealing an inverse correlation between excessive zinc and insulin expression, suggestive of zinc-mediated insulin expression suppression. In addition, we found that zinc levels in $1 1 \mathsf { S } ^ { + } \mathrm { ~ \beta ~ }$ cells are significantly higher than those in $\mathsf { G C G } ^ { + } \mathrm { ~ \pmb ~ { \alpha } ~ }$ cells (Figure S1B), consistent with their distinct roles in hormone synthesis and secretion. In β cells, zinquin-labeled zinc is predominantly localized within insulin granules, 48, 49 with partial overlap with the Golgi apparatus, indicating that zinc is mainly enriched in Golgi-associated compartments and insulin granules rather than in the endoplasmic reticulum (Figure S1C). Current studies report that zinc induces reactive oxygen species (ROS) accumulation,50 which drives AMPK oxidation and aggregation.51 To validate this mechanism in SC-islets, we applied zinc ionophore-zinc pyrithione (ZnPTO, a membranepermeable zinc coordination complex52,53) to mimic pathologically elevated zinc transportation. ZnPTO treatment triggered zinc-dependent ROS elevation (Figures 1J and 1K), while zincenriched regions showed diminished AMPKα expression54 (Figures 1L and 1M). Furthermore, ZnPTO treatment exacerbated AMPKα oxidation, providing mechanistic evidence for zinc-mediated AMPK inactivation (Figures 1N and 1O). Collectively, these findings suggest dysregulated zinc homeostasis as a key driver of AMPK inactivation in SC-islets, positioning zinc transportation inhibition as a viable strategy to enhance AMPK activity and functional maturation.

# Chemical inhibition of zinc transportation prevents AMPK oxidation in SC-islets

To identify compounds capable of reducing zinc transportation and consequently enhancing AMPK activity and functional maturation in SC-islets, we performed compound screening and validation of zinc transportation inhibitors using both SC-islets and human primary islets as experimental models. In the first screening phase, we seeded INSGFP/W-NKX6.1mCherry/mCherry Mel1-R-derived SC-islets into 96-well plates, followed by the treatment of compounds from a small-molecule library, including 1,685 chemical inhibitors. Following 48-h incubation, zinc levels were quantified via zinquin-based fluorescence imaging, with decreased zinquin fluorescence intensity serving as the primary readout for zinc transportation inhibition (Figures 2A and S1D). From the screening, SU6656 (25 nM) emerged as a candidate, effectively inhibiting zinc transportation in wild-type (WT) SC-islets while increasing the proportion of $W { \sf S } ^ { + } N { \sf K } \times 6 . 1 ^ { + } \ \beta$ cells in Mel1-R-derived SC-islets and C-peptide $^ { + } NKX6.1+$ β cells in HuES8- or AD-B-derived SC-islets, with appropriate controls included (Figures 2B–2D, S1E, and S1F). After SU6656 treatment, which reduces zinc transportation, the number of INS-GFP strong cells significantly increased, while INS-GFP weak cells markedly decreased (Figures S1G and S1H), revealing SC-islet β cell heterogeneity based on the diversity of zinc accumulation, which is consistent with our previous study with human islets.55 Its zinc transportation inhibitory activity was first confirmed in human primary islets (Figure S1I). To further rigorously validate the zinc transportation inhibitory efficacy of SU6656, we employed the genetic (WFS1 knockout [KO], amplifying zinc transportation55) and ZnPTO-treated SCislets as the elevated zinc transportation models. Importantly, SU6656 effectively normalized zinc transportation in both pathological models, establishing its robust inhibition of zinc transportation (Figures S1J–S1M). Next, we examined whether the zinc transportation inhibitor can activate AMPK in zinc-overloaded model; we found that SU6656 reversed ZnPTO-treatment-induced ROS accumulation (Figures 2E and S2A), prevented AMPK oxidation (Figures 2F and S2B–S2D), and restored AMPK activation (Figures 2G and S2E). Furthermore, given that zinc transportation in β cells is predominantly mediated by the zinc transporter ZnT8,56 we employed ZnT8 KO to abolish zinc transportation. Consistent with chemical treatment of SU6656, compared with WT SC-islets, $S L C 3 0 A 8 ^ { - / - }$ SC-islets with abolished zinc transportation43 also showed elevated AMPK activity, while $W F S 1 ^ { - / - }$ SC-islets with elevated zinc transportation55,57 displayed reduced AMPK activation (Figures S2F–S2J). Collectively, these data further confirmed that zinc transportation inhibition promoted AMPK activity. Importantly, SU6656 treatment effectively increased AMPK activity not only in WT SC-islets (Figures 2H and S2H) but also in zinc-overloaded model $W F S 1 ^ { - / - }$ SC-islets (Figure S2J). However, in $S L C 3 0 A 8 ^ { - / - }$ SC-islets where zinc transportation was abolished, SU6656 failed to amplify additional AMPK activation (Figures 2H, S2F, and S2H). These results suggest that SU6656’s AMPK-activating effect in SC-islets was dependent on zinc transportation inhibition. # SU6656 accelerates SC-islet functional maturation through AMPK activation

Since β cell maturation is typically accompanied by metabolic adaptation and mitochondrial functional optimization, we next investigated whether SU6656 could promote SC-islet maturation via AMPK activation.61 To test this, we performed scRNAseq and snATAC-seq analysis on DMSOor SU6656-treated SC-islets (Figure 3A). The scRNA-seq and snATAC-seq data were integrated based on gene expression profiling and gene activity scores. Using dimensionality reduction and unsupervised clustering, we resolved distinct cellular clusters, which were then annotated as six cell types based on using established markers: α cells (GCG), β cells (INS), δ cells (SST), enterochromaffin cells (FEV), progenitor cells (SOX9), and proliferating cells (TOP2A) (Figures 3B and S3A–S3C). The resulting SC-islet cellular composition resembled human islet heterogeneity.62 Specifically, SU6656 treatment increased the β cell population (Figures 3C, S3D, and S3E), directly extending our earlier observation of its ability to increase $W { \sf S } ^ { + } \sf N K X 6 . 1 ^ { + } \beta$ cells (Figure 2D). Notably, single-cell transcriptomic analysis revealed a marked reduction in SLC30A8 expression following SU6656 treatment (Figure S3F), indicating that the inhibitory effect of SU6656 on zinc transportation is mediated through reducing transcriptional suppression of SLC30A8. To further validate the inhibitory mechanism of SU6656 on ZnT8, we utilized a Mel1 $( I N S ^ { w / G F P } )$ - ZnT8::RFP-reporter line, enabling visualization of ZnT8 expression and localization as a fusion protein. Following SU6656 treatment, both ZnT8 mRNA levels and ZnT8::RFP fluorescence intensity markedly decreased, suggesting that SU6656 suppresses ZnT8 expression (Figures S3G–S3J).

To further determine whether SU6656 can activate AMPK independently of SLC30A8, we next examined its effect in the absence of functional ZnT8. To test the specificity of SU6656 on AMPK activation by inhibition of ZnT8-mediated zinc transportation, we found that SU6656, acting as a chemical inhibitor of zinc transportation, specifically blocked ZnT8- mediated zinc transportation (Figures S3K and S3L). Genetic abolishment of zinc transportation (by SLC30A8/ZnT8 KO) leads to AMPK activation, suggesting an inhibitory role of zinc transportation. Consistently, chemical inhibition of zinc transportation (SU6656 treatment) also elevates AMPK activity, and additionally, SU6656 counteracts the inhibitory role by excessive zinc on AMPK activation. Moreover, SU6656 no longer produced additional AMPK activation in the $S L C 3 0 A 8 ^ { - / - }$ background (Figure S3M), demonstrating that SU6656 specifically targets ZnT8-mediated zinc transportation to activate AMPK. To further establish whether SU6656 exerts hypoxia resistance through AMPK activation, we examined the interaction between SU6656 and AMPK signaling under hypoxia. Under normoxia, DS treatment elevated HIF1A levels and increased 7-AAD+ cell death, whereas SU6656 rescued both phenotypes by reducing HIF1A expression and cell death (Figures S4O– S4R). Under hypoxic conditions, DS treatment markedly exacerbated SC-islet cell death, indicating that suppression of AMPK phosphorylation severely compromises hypoxia tolerance. Notably, co-treatment with SU6656 substantially rescued this DS-induced vulnerability, significantly reducing hypoxia-associated cell death and restoring SC-islet viability (Figures S4S and S4T) These findings indicate that SU6656 can effectively counteract the detrimental effects of AMPK inhibition under hypoxic stress. In contrast, SC-islets treated with DS alone exhibited the highest level of cell death among all experimental groups, underscoring the essential role of AMPK activity in maintaining SC-islet survival under hypoxic conditions. AMPK de-activation induced by DS treatment markedly reduced VEGFA expression, demonstrating that AMPK activation is required for VEGFA expression in SC-islets (Figure S3M). Collectively, these results show that inhibiting AMPK phosphorylation using DS diminishes SC-islet maturation, weakens hypoxia resistance, and decreases VEGFA expression, jointly confirming that AMPK activation is the central mechanism through which SU6656 achieves its multifaceted benefits. Notably, hypoxia stabilizes HIF1A to activate VEGFA-driven angiogenesis in diverse cell types, including in tumor cells and decidual endometrial stromal cells.88–90 However, given the vulnerability of SC-islets to hypoxia, it is imperative to bypass the HIF1A-mediated adaptive phase while inducing VEGFA expression to accelerate vascularization. Importantly, HIF1A-independent VEGFA activation has also been reported in other cell types, such as muscle cells, where alternative transcriptional programs drive angiogenesis independently of canonical hypoxia signaling.91 Although HIF1A has been traditionally reported as a central mediator of VEGFA expression in multiple tissues under hypoxic conditions, we observed an apparent dissociation between HIF1A and VEGFA regulation in SU6656-treated SC-islets: HIF1A expression was reduced, whereas VEGFA expression was markedly increased. This observation indicates the existence of an HIF1A-independent pathway driving VEGFA activation.

To identify transcriptional regulators responsible for VEGFA activation independent of HIF1A, we used chromVAR to predict TF activity changes and identified SU6656-induced activation of TFs. As a result, ETS192 was identified, which is a direct regulator of VEGFA based on JASPAR motif analysis93 (Figure 4H). To directly test the hypothesis that SU6656 induced HIF1A-independent VEGFA upregulation, SC-islets were treated with the HIF1A inhibitor YC-194,95 under hypoxic conditions $( 1 \% 0 _ { 2 } )$ ). Notably, VEGFA expression remained significantly elevated upon SU6656 treatment despite pharmacological inhibition of HIF1A. Consistently, ETS1 overexpression (ETS1 OE) also increased VEGFA levels under hypoxia in the presence of YC-1. These results demonstrate that in hypoxic SC-islets, SU6656 activates VEGFA expression through an HIF1A-independent mechanism (Figure 4I). We further examined VEGFA expression in SC-islets following either SU6656 treatment or genetic ablation of SLC30A8 and observed significant upregulation of VEGFA in both conditions (Figures 4J, 4K, and S4U–S4X); importantly, SU6656 treatment also robustly elevated VEGFA expression in human primary islets (Figure S4Y), further supporting a zinc transport-dependent but HIF1A-independent regulatory axis. Given VEGFA’s crucial role in vascularization, we further assessed its functional impact by co-culturing SC-islets with human umbilical vein endothelial cells (HUVECs). The HUVECs exhibited network formation, suggesting enhanced vascularization potential. To quantitatively evaluate vascular network growth in the co-culture system, we measured key morphometric parameters, including the number of branches, junctions, total length, and total segment length (Figure S4Z). Compared with the DMSO-treated group, the SU6656-treated SC-islets showed a significant increase in all these parameters, further supporting the propelling role of SU6656 in SC-islet vascularization. In summary, our results demonstrate that SU6656 acts as a chemical inhibitor of zinc transportation to activate AMPK and bypass the lethal HIF1A-dependent hypoxia adaptation phase in SC-islet transplantation while promoting HIF1A-independent VEGFA expression, thereby potentially accelerating angiogenesis during the critical early post-transplantation window (Figure 4L).

# SU6656 protects SC-islets from apoptosis induced by metabolic stress

Beyond overcoming short-term ischemic stress and promoting angiogenesis, the long-term survival and functional maintenance of SC-islets under metabolic stress are equally crucial.96 Previous studies have demonstrated that diabetic conditions upregulate heat shock protein 90 (HSP90) expression, and its inhibition ameliorates metabolic stress. In order to explore whether SU6656 can protect SC-islets from metabolic stress, we identified stress-responsive pathways functionally coupled to zinc transportation networks based on our single-cell transcriptomic profiling. SU6656 treatment caused coordinated downregulation of HSP90-related and apoptotic pathways, including decreased expression of HSP90AA1 (involved in heat shock response) and CYCS (critical for apoptosome formation) (Figures S3T and S5A–S5C). Notably, HSP90 dissociates heat shock factor 1 (HSF1), enabling its activation and subsequent initiation of pro-apoptotic signaling cascades under stress condition.11 HSP90α and its phosphorylation at Thr5/7 sites are directly involved in apoptosis regulation in various cancer cell lines, demonstrating their functional participation in cell apoptosis.97 Experimental validation confirmed that SU6656 reduced basal expression of HSP90AA1 (encode HSP90) expression and suppressed zinc overloadinduced HSP90AA1 upregulation (Figures S5D and S5E). To verify whether SU6656 could inhibit HSP90α Thr5/7 phosphorylation by reducing zinc transportation, we examined two independent models with elevated zinc transportation: ZnPTO-treated WT SC-islets and genetic zinc-overloaded $W F S 1 ^ { - / - }$ SC-islets. SU6656 consistently suppressed zinc transportation-elevated HSP90α Thr5/7 phosphorylation (Figures S5G and S5H). Collectively, these findings suggest that SU6656 protects SC-islets from apoptosis through reducing HSP90α expression and suppressing its Thr5/7 phosphorylation (Figure S5I).

To determine whether SU6656 reduces metabolic stressinduced apoptosis in SC-islets, we first assessed apoptosis levels in untreated WT SC-islets and found that SU6656 treatment not only enhanced SC-islet maturation but also significantly decreased apoptosis in mature β cells (Figures 2D and S5J). To extend these findings to conditions of zinc accumulation, we examined two models with elevated zinc transportation and found that SU6656 consistently reduced apoptosis in both models, demonstrating the broad efficacy of SU6656 in counteracting zinc transportation-induced apoptotic pathways (Figures S5K–S5M). Then, we investigated whether SU6656 could protect SC-islets from apoptosis under lipotoxicity, glucotoxicity, and oxidative stress. We treated SC-islets with palmitic acid (PA), stearic acid (SA), linoleic acid (LA), D-glucose (Glu), and ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$, and observed significantly reduced apoptosis with SU6656 treatment (Figures S5N–S5S). In the end, a similar protective effect was further validated in human primary islets under excessive zinc, lipotoxicity, and glucotoxicity (Figures S5T–S5X). Collectively, these results demonstrate that SU6656 protects SC-islets against metabolic stress and inhibits HSP90α expression and phosphorylation-dependent apoptotic signaling.

# SU6656 pre-adapts SC-islets to hypoxia for angiogenesis

Early post-transplant hypoxia drives substantial β cell loss in grafts through mechanisms involving ROS accumulation and endoplasmic reticulum stress, particularly under hyperglycemia-induced exacerbation.10,29,32,97 Our in vitro studies demonstrate that SU6656 enhances SC-islet functional maturation via zinc transportation inhibition while concurrently improving hypoxia tolerance and protecting against diabetic microenvironment-induced SC-islet loss. Furthermore, by upregulating VEGFA expression to support angiogenesis, these combined effects may collectively address the ischemic phase-driven graft failure, thereby improving post-transplant survival and functional outcomes. To validate the efficacy in vivo, Mel1-R- and AD-Bderived SC-islets were preconditioned with 25 nM SU6656 for 7 days (from days 7 to 14 at stage 7) and were transplanted under the left renal capsule of streptozotocin (STZ)-induced hyperglycemic SCID-beige mice (blood glucose $> 2 2 . 2$ mmol/L) (Figure 5A). At 1 and 2 weeks post-transplantation, immunofluorescence analysis of transplanted kidneys revealed significantly reduced HIF1A expression but elevated graft survival and VEGFA levels in the SU6656-preconditioned group, accompanied by increased CD31+ vascularization areas (Figures 5B– 5G). These results indicated that SU6656 broke the hypoxiadriven paradox (hypoxia marker HIF1A stabilization induced VEGFA expression vs. SC-islet loss under hypoxia due to lack of vascularization) by enhancing hypoxia tolerance and VEGFA expression of SC-islets to improve graft vascularization and survival in diabetic mice.

# SU6656 preconditioning improves glycemic restoration in diabetic mice

Our results have shown that SU6656-preconditioned SC-islets exhibit significant advantages in the early stages of transplantation. However, the long-term diabetic microenvironment can further lead to hypoxia, insufficient VEGFA expression, and decreased cell viability in the later stages of grafts.10,31 We further investigated whether SU6656- preconditioned SC-islets sustained long-term functional efficacy by resolving the paradoxical interplay between hypoxia-induced VEGFA expression and early graft loss. To assess sustained efficacy, we evaluated Mel1-R- and AD-B-derived grafts 14 weeks post-transplantation, revealing that SU6656-preconditioned SC-islets maintained higher viability; elevated INS+ cell proportion; and enhanced maturity markers such as PDX1, SYT13, NKX6.1, MAFA, ISL1, and PC1/3 (Figures 6A–6D and S6A–S6H). Histological examination revealed that SU6656-preconditioned grafts exhibited a compact, homogeneous morphology without regions of ductal expansion (Figure S6N), consistent with previous studies demonstrating complete differentiation and functional maturation3 ; moreover, kidney endogenous vascular structure in the host compartment was also observed showing the clear vascular nature of the kidney (Figure S6O). In addition to enhancing the intrinsic maturation of SC-islets, we investigated SU6656’s effects on hypoxia resistance and vascularization in grafts at 14 weeks post-transplantation. Our results demonstrated significantly upregulated VEGFA expression and enhanced blood vessel formation (Figures 6E, 6F, S6I, and S6J), along with improved hypoxia tolerance as evidenced by downregulated HIF1A expression (Figures S6P and S6Q). These results suggest that zinc transportation inhibited by SU6656 accelerates vascularization after overcoming hypoxic stress to improve graft viability. Next, we proceeded to conduct functional analysis of the grafts. Our results revealed that hyperglycemic mice transplanted with SU6656-preconditioned SC-islets demonstrated earlier random blood glucose restoration (random blood glucose level <10 mmol/L at 7 weeks post-transplantation) and exhibited elevated levels of glucose-stimulated human insulin and C-peptide secretion in vivo at 4, 8, and 14 weeks (Figures 6G– 6J, S6K, S6L, S6R, and S6S). We reveal a regulatory axis in which zinc accumulation in SC- β cells suppresses AMPK activity, a defect that sustains metabolic immaturity and severely impairs hypoxia adaptation. Our results demonstrate that inhibited zinc transportation leads to AMPK activation, conferring the hypoxia resistance in SC-islets. Chemical inhibitor of zinc transportation enables HIF1A-independent VEGFA expression even under hypoxia, thereby accelerating graft revascularization while preserving β cell survival. By reconciling the dual demands of metabolic robustness and rapid vascular integration, our findings demonstrate that AMPK activation by chemical inhibition of zinc transportation boosts SC-islet functional competence, offering a potential strategy to improve the efficacy of stem cell-based therapies for diabetes. Improving SC-islet functionality requires hierarchical optimization of differentiation protocols, structural mimicry, and metabolic adaptation. Recent progress includes refined differentiation timelines that enhance dynamic insulin secretion through small-molecule-mediated multi-pathway modulation, coupled with 3D architectural optimization to replicate native islet glucose responsiveness.2,3,6,60,61,101–105 Metabolic maturation strategies addressing mitochondrial energetics and glucose metabolism coupling show promise in resolving insulin secretion deficits, such as Wnt pathway modulation and ceramide homeostasis remodeling,4,106–108 to improve lineage specificity and SC-islet β cell maturation. Complementary strategies like circadian rhythm synchronization further align secretory and metabolic cycles.109 Yet, these approaches do not address hypoxia tolerance and vascularization post-transplantation for SC-islets. Significant progress has been made in enhancing graft vascularization through bioengineering-based strategies. Recent advancements include 3D-printed vascular scaffolds with angiogenic microchannels, hypoxia-responsive nanoparticles for controlled VEGF delivery, and endothelial cell-laden bioinks that promote rapid anastomosis with host vasculature. $7, 1 0, 1 1 0 { - } 1 1 9$ While bioengineering innovations have pioneered structural solutions for graft vascularization, their reliance on external scaffolds introduces inherent complexities in clinical scalability. In parallel, emerging pharmacological strategies that potentiate endogenous signaling pathways offer a complementary approach, coupling mechanistic simplicity with physiological adaptability.120 Here, we demonstrate that chemical inhibition of zinc transportation in SC-islets unveils a self-sustaining vascularization mechanism; AMPK activation not only confers hypoxia resistance but also amplifies endogenous VEGFA expression, which accelerates graft revascularization and enhances glycemic control in diabetic models, without reliance on external scaffolds, invasive methods, or complex bioengineering. In the present study, our mechanistic analyses demonstrate that SU6656 activates AMPK in SC-islets through a zinc-dependent and ZnT8-specific pathway. Genetic ablation of SLC30A8 is sufficient to abolish zinc transportation and activate AMPK, phenocopying the effect of SU6656 treatment. Importantly, in $S L C 3 0 A 8 ^ { - / - }$ SC-islets, where zinc transportation is largely eliminated, SU6656 fails to further enhance AMPK phosphorylation, suggesting that its AMPK-activating capacity in SC-islets critically depends on inhibiting zinc transportation. These findings establish zinc homeostasis, rather than direct kinase engagement, as the dominant regulatory node through which SU6656 modulates AMPK signaling in SC-islets. Notably, SU6656 has been reported to activate AMPK through alternative mechanisms in other cell types, including Fyn kinase inhibition in murine skeletal muscle121 and direct ATP-competitive binding to the AMPK kinase domain in HEK293 cells,122 which facilitates LKB1-mediated Thr172 phosphorylation independently of cellular energy status. The apparent divergence among these mechanisms likely reflects cell-type-specific signaling architectures and metabolic dependencies. Together, our data indicate that while SU6656 may engage distinct molecular targets in non- β cell systems, its primary mode of AMPK activation in SC-islets is mediated by inhibition of ZnT8-dependent zinc transportation.

# scRNA-seq data analysis

# scRNA-seq on WT SC-islets

Raw sequencing data were aligned to the GRCh38 human reference genome. Low-quality cells were filtered out by applying the following thresholds: cells with fewer than 200 or more than 9,000 detected genes, and cells with mitochondrial gene content exceeding 10% . Genes expressed in fewer than 3 cells per sample were excluded. We normalized the data using the SCTransform v2 method. SelectIntegrationFeatures was used to set variable features. Principal component analysis was performed using the RunPCA function, with the top 40 PCs retained for cell clustering and downstream analysis. FindNeighbors and FindClusters (the resolution was adjusted to 0.1) functions were run. We performed dimensionality reduction using the RunUMAP function.

To define cell types, marker genes for each cluster were identified with the FindAllMarkers function. Based on the expression of known marker genes (GCG, INS, SST, FEV, SOX9, TOP2A), the distinct clusters were identified and annotated as α-cells, β-cells, δ-cells, enterochromaffin cells, progenitor cells, and proliferating cells. We compared the differentially expressed genes (DEGs) levels in β-cells between DMSO and SU6656. DEGs were identified using FindMarkers function in Seurat with adjusted $p$ -value < 0.05 and |log2(fold change) $> 0 . 1$ as cutoffs. Pathway enrichment analysis was performed using the GO and Reactome databases via the R package clusterProfiler.

# snATAC-seq data analysis

Sequencing reads were processed through the Cell Ranger ATAC pipeline and aligned to the GRCh38-1.1.0 reference genome for mapping, de-duplication, and identification of Tn5 cut sites. The objects were generated from Signac. We kept cells of which mapped ATAC-seq fragments more than 500 and less than 1200, TSS enrichment score more than 4 and the approximate ratio of mononucleosomal to nucleosome-free fragments less than 1.2. The doublet score was calculated by scDblFinder package using scDblFinder function. We performed latent semantic indexing (LSI) to reduce dimension. We clustered cells and added UMAP embedding through FindNeighbors and FindClusters.

sn ATAC-seq and scRNA-seq dataset were integrated. We initially estimated gene activity scores from snATAC-seq data using the GeneActivity function, followed by log-normalization. To integrate the datasets, we compared the gene activity scores derived from snATAC-seq with gene expression profiles from scRNA-seq. This was achieved using the IntegrateLayers function, which employed Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) for dimension reduction and alignment. We identified cell types based on gene markers mentioned below. Differential accessible genes (DAGs) were calculated using FindMarkers function based on gene activity scores.

# Transcription factor activity analysis

Transcription factor activity was performed using chromVAR. The position weight matrix was obtained from JASPAR2020 database. Per-cell motif activity score was computed by RunChromVAR function in Signac. We calculated differential activity by using FindMarkers function.

# SC-islet live imaging

96-well plates (Thermo Scientific Nunc, #167008) were coated with Matrigel (1:3 dilution in DMEM/F12) and incubated at 37°C for 30 minutes to polymerize. SC-islets were resuspended in stage 7 medium, and an equal number was added to each pre-coated well, followed by a 6-hour settling period at 37°C in a humidified 5% CO₂ incubator. After incubation, plates were transferred to a live-cell imaging system (Zeiss Celldiscoverer 7) pre-equilibrated at 37°C and 5% CO₂ to maintain physiological conditions. INS-GFP fluorescence was captured every 3 hours for 21 hours. Fluorescence intensity of INS-GFP was quantified using ImageJ software by outlining the entire SC-islets.

# TUNEL staining

Slides were permeabilized with proteinase $\mathsf { K } ( 2 0 \mu \mathsf { g } / \mathsf { m L } )$ for 10 minutes at room temperature. After washing, slides were equilibrated in $1 \times$ reaction buffer for 25 minutes. Terminal deoxynucleotidyl transferase (TdT)-mediated dUTP nick-end labeling was then carried out by incubating sections with a TdT enzyme/YSFluor™488-dUTP reaction mix (Yeasen, #40306ES20) at 37°C for 60 minutes in the dark. The reaction was terminated by washing with PBS. Subsequently, slides were blocked and incubated with primary antibodies overnight at 4°C, followed by species-matched fluorescent secondary antibodies for 45 minutes at room temperature. Cell nuclei were stained with DAPI, and coverslips were mounted. Fluorescent images were acquired using an Olympus FV3000 confocal microscope.
