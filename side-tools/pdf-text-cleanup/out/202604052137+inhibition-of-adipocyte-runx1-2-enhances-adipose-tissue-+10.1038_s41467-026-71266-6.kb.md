# Introduction

Obesity and its associated metabolic disorders continue to represent major global health challenges<sup>1</sup>. Central to these conditions is an imbalance in energy homeostasis, characterized by excessive caloric intake coupled with insufficient energy expenditure<sup>2, 3</sup>. Thermogenic adipocytes, including classical brown adipocytes and inducible beige adipocytes, are characterized by multiple small lipid droplets, abundant mitochondria, and elevated content of uncoupling protein 1 (UCP1), a key thermogenic marker<sup>4, 5</sup>. Notably, thermogenic adipocytes play crucial roles in maintaining energy balance due to their distinctive ability to dissipate excess energy through heat production, a process known as adaptive thermogenesis<sup>6</sup>. Enhancing adipocyte thermogenic capacity has thus emerged as a promising strategy to counteract obesity and its metabolic sequelae<sup>7, 8</sup>. While significant advances have been made in unraveling the molecular landscape governing adipocyte biology, our comprehensive understanding of the transcriptional and epigenetic regulatory networks controlling thermogenic adipocyte differentiation and function remains incomplete ⁹,¹⁰ . Key transcription factors and co-activators, such as peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor γ (PPAR γ ), PR/SET domain family 16 (PRDM16), early B-cell factor 2 (EBF2), peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor γ coactivator-1 α (PGC1 α ), and members of the C/EBP family, have been identified as pivotal positive regulators of the thermogenic gene program ¹¹,¹²,¹³ . In addition, several negative regulators, including zinc finger protein 423 (ZFP423) and nuclear receptor interacting protein 1 (NRIP1), have also been recognized for their roles in suppressing adipose browning ¹⁴,¹⁵ . Notably, recent studies utilizing CRISPR-based targeting of NRIP1 have demonstrated enhanced human adipocyte browning, suggesting significant therapeutic potential as a cell-based treatment for metabolic diseases $^{16}$ . Moreover, epigenetic modifiers also play crucial roles; for instance, histone deacetylase 1 (HDAC1) represses brown fat-specific gene activation by removing acetylation and promoting methylation of histone H3 lysine 27 (H3K27), while HDAC3 uniquely primes Ucp1 and the thermogenic transcriptional network to sustain thermogenesis in brown adipose tissue ¹⁷,¹⁸,¹⁹ . Thus, a deeper understanding of these transcriptional and epigenetic mechanisms is essential for fully leveraging thermogenic adipocytes as therapeutic targets for metabolic diseases. The Runt-related transcription factors (RUNX) family, encompassing RUNX1, RUNX2 and RUNX3, are well-established critical transcriptional modulators involved in diverse biological processes such as hematopoiesis, osteogenesis and immune regulation $^{20}$ . Yet, their functions in adipocyte biology and energy metabolism remain poorly defined. Previous studies suggest a potential inhibitory role of RUNX2 in white adipocyte differentiation, as its deletion induces white adipogenic phenotypes in non-adipogenic lineages $^{21}$ . Conversely, RUNX1 may play a supportive role in thermogenic adipocyte activation, as cyclin dependent kinase 6 (CDK6) has been shown to suppress adipose browning by inhibiting RUNX1 $^{22}$ . Additionally, both RUNX1 and RUNX2 expression levels are upregulated during the differentiation of human pluripotent stem cells into brown adipocytes $^{23}$ . These findings highlight the complexity of RUNX1/2 function in adipose tissue and underscore the need for further investigation into their precise roles and regulatory mechanisms in cell fate determination of thermogenic adipocyte.

In this study, we employed unbiased epigenetic profiling during brown adipocyte differentiation and identified RUNX1 and RUNX2 as key transcriptional barriers to thermogenic fat activation. Genetic deletion of RUNX1 or RUNX2 in adipocytes enhanced beige fat formation but differentially influenced systemic metabolism. Mechanistically, RUNX1 recruited HDAC1 to mediate epigenetic suppression near thermogenic genes, whereas RUNX2 governed thermogenic cell fate through phase separation. Notably, pharmacological inhibition of RUNX1/2 enhanced adipose thermogenesis and improved energy metabolism. Importantly, the expression level of RUNX1 was strongly positively correlated with obesity and adverse metabolic traits. These findings provide mechanistic insights into how RUNX1 and RUNX2 contribute to the regulation of adipose thermogenesis and offer potential therapeutic avenues to combat obesity and metabolic disorders.

# Results

# Reduced enrichment and expression of RUNX1/2 during adipose thermogenesis

To uncover novel transcriptional regulatory mechanisms of brown adipogenesis, we performed Assay for Transposase-Accessible Chromatin using sequencing (ATAC-seq) to map genome-wide chromatin accessibility during the early stages of immortalized primary brown preadipocytes differentiation[24]. Our analysis identified 41,449 ATAC peaks in brown preadipocytes and 26,348 peaks in differentiated brown adipocytes at day 1 (D1), with the majority of peaks overlapping between these two states (Fig. 1a and Supplementary Fig. 1a). Notably, we observed a loss of 16,827 peaks during differentiation, while 1,726 peaks emerged specifically at D1. De novo motif analysis confirmed the expected enrichment of CEBP, NFIX, and RXR motifs in D1-specific open chromatin regions $^{25}$ (Fig. 1b, c). Intriguingly, we identified a significant enrichment of the RUNX transcription factor binding motif within brown preadipocyte-specific peaks, suggesting a previously unrecognized role of the RUNX family as a potential barrier to brown adipogenesis. Given the low expression level of Runx3 in the preadipocyte cell line (Supplementary Fig. 1b), we focused on investigating the role of RUNX1 and RUNX2 in thermogenic fat cell fate determination. Our analysis revealed that RUNX1 and RUNX2 mRNA and protein levels were significantly downregulated during brown adipogenesis (Fig. 1d-f and Supplementary Fig. 1c). Furthermore, to assess whether this pattern was conserved in beige adipogenesis, we isolated the stromal vascular fraction (SVF) from subcutaneous adipose tissue and induced beige adipocyte differentiation. Notably, we observed a similar downregulation of RUNX1 and RUNX2 during beige adipocyte differentiation (Fig. 1g-i and Supplementary Fig. 1d).

# RUNX1/2 decrease with thermal stress and correlate with adverse metabolic traits

Across three distinct adipose depots, brown adipose tissue (BAT), inguinal white adipose tissue (iWAT), and epididymal white adipose tissue (eWAT), we observed that RUNX1 and RUNX2 mRNA and protein expression levels were significantly repressed in BAT compared to iWAT and eWAT (Fig. 1j-1 and Supplementary Fig. 1e), suggesting the potential negative regulatory role of RUNX1/2 in thermogenesis. To further investigate the role of RUNX1/2 in adaptive thermogenesis, we examined expression dynamics of RUNX1/2 under thermal stress. One week of cold exposure robustly induced the thermogenic marker UCP1 in iWAT, confirming the activation of cold-induced thermogenesis (Fig. 1m, n and Supplementary Fig. 1f). Upon subsequent acclimation to thermoneutrality (TN), UCP1 expression gradually declined over the course of one to two weeks, demonstrating reversible metabolic remodeling. In contrast, RUNX1 and RUNX2 expression levels were significantly repressed in iWAT under cold exposure and restored after thermoneutral acclimation, whereas their expression in BAT remained at near-undetectable levels across all temperature conditions (Fig. 1m, n and Supplementary Fig. 1f, g). Furthermore, cell-type-specific analysis revealed that RUNX1/2 dynamically responded to cold exposure in adipose progenitors and mature adipocytes, but not in immune cell populations (Supplementary Fig. 1h-m), suggesting their regulation is primarily restricted to the adipocyte lineage $^{26}$ . Similarly, RUNX1/2 proteins levels were also downregulated in iWAT in CL316243 (CL, a $\beta 3$ -adrenergic receptor agonist)-induced browning model (Fig. 1o, p). These data suggest RUNX1/2 expression is repressed during white fat browning.

Conversely, in models of diet-induced obesity, we observed an increase of RUNX1 protein level in iWAT, while RUNX2 exhibited only slight reduction (Fig. 1q, r). Interestingly, both RUNX1 and RUNX2 expression levels were decreased in eWAT, with no appreciable changes detected in BAT, suggesting depot-specific regulation of RUNX1/2 expression in response to nutritional stress (Supplementary Fig. 2a, b). Moreover, cell-type-specific analysis revealed that RUNX1 expression increased, whereas RUNX2 decreased, in response to HFD specifically in adipose progenitors and mature adipocytes, but not in immune cell populations (Supplementary Fig. 2c-f). These findings indicate that the nutritional stress-driven regulation of RUNX1/2 is largely restricted to the adipocyte lineage, consistent with the expression patterns observed in whole iWAT tissue (Fig. 1q, r).

To further elucidate the relationship between RUNX1/2 and obesity, we analyzed their mRNA expression in abdominal adipose tissues of human patients, alongside circulating metabolite profiles, using the publicly available ATportal database $^{27}$ . Our analysis revealed that RUNX1 expression was positively correlated with waist-to-hip ratio (WHR), waist circumference, and BMI in humans (Fig. 1s). Furthermore, elevated RUNX1 level was significantly associated with markers of metabolic dysfunction, including HOMA-IR, insulin, triglycerides, C-reactive protein (CRP), glucose, and low-density lipoprotein cholesterol (LDL-C), while showing a negative correlation with high-density lipoprotein cholesterol (HDL-C) (Fig. 1s). Notably, multiple cohort studies indicated that RUNX1 expression was consistently elevated in obese individuals ²⁷,²⁸,²⁹ (Supplementary Fig. 3a). Following weight loss induced by bariatric surgery, RUNX1 mRNA levels gradually declined (Fig. 1t), suggesting a dynamic link between RUNX1 and metabolic status. In contrast, RUNX2 mRNA levels showed weaker associations with metabolic traits and exhibited only mild fluctuations in both obese individuals and those who had undergone weight loss interventions ²⁸,³⁰ (Supplementary Fig. 3b-d). Further analysis of a recently published snRNA-seq dataset on human subcutaneous adipose tissue during obesity and therapeutic weight loss revealed that RUNX1 expression in adipocytes and adipocyte progenitors positively correlated with BMI, whereas no significant associations were observed in immune cell populations $^{31}$ (Supplementary Fig. 3e-h). RUNX2 expression showed no obvious correlation with BMI across any cell type, potentially reflecting its relatively low expression levels in adipose tissue. Together, we hypothesized that RUNX1/2 may serve as a potential regulator in adipocytes thermogenesis and energy metabolism, thereby contributing to the development of obesity-associated metabolic dysfunction.

# RUNX1/2 regulate thermogenic adipocyte differentiation and activation

To investigate the role of RUNX1/2 in brown adipogenesis and thermogenesis, we overexpressed RUNX1 (OE-RUNX1) and RUNX2 (OE-RUNX2) in brown preadipocytes and induced their differentiation into mature brown adipocytes (Supplementary Fig. 4a, b). RUNX1/2 overexpression markedly inhibited lipiddroplet accumulation and reduced total cellular triglyceride (TG) content, accompanied by decreased expression of both early adipogenic $(Dlk1, Ly6a)$ and pan-adipogenic marker genes (Cebpd, Cebpb, Cebpa, Pparg2, Adipoq and Fabp4) (Supplementary Fig. 4c-f), suggesting that RUNX1/2 overexpression impairs adipogenic differentiation. Consistently, RUNX1/2 overexpression substantially decreased the protein abundance of the master adipogenic regulator PPARγ (Supplementary Fig. 4g-j). Transcriptomic analysis revealed distinct gene expression profiles in response to RUNX1/2 overexpression (Supplementary Fig. 4k). Gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) of the most variable principal component (PC1) showed significant enrichment of signaling pathways related to aerobic respiration, thermogenesis and oxidative phosphorylation (Supplementary Fig. 4l). Notably, we identified a substantial number of genes that were downregulated upon RUNX1 or RUNX2 overexpression, with a strong overlap between OE-RUNX1 and OE-RUNX2 groups (Fig. 2a and Supplementary Fig. 4m). Gene ontology (GO) analysis indicated that these overlapping downregulated genes were highly enriched in thermogenic pathways, including brown fat cell differentiation and adaptive thermogenesis (Fig. 2b and Supplementary Fig. 4n, o). Notably, we noticed that RUNX2 exerted a stronger inhibitory effect on thermogenic gene expression than RUNX1 (Supplementary Fig. 4p). Specifically, key thermogenic genes such as Ucp1, Elovl3, Cox8b, and Cidea were significantly downregulated in both OE-RUNX1 and OE-RUNX2 groups, a finding further validated by RT-qPCR (Fig. 2c, d). These results suggest that RUNX1/2 overexpression suppresses critical functions associated with brown adipocyte biology, particularly thermogenesis and cellular respiration. In line with this, RUNX1/2 overexpression markedly reduced mitochondrial abundance and downregulated key components of the mitochondrial respiratory complexes as well as UCP1, with RUNX2 overexpression producing a more pronounced phenotype (Fig. 2e-h). Consistently, mitochondrial stress testing, as measured by the oxygen consumption rate (OCR) in basal and isoproterenol-stimulated states, revealed impaired mitochondrial function following RUNX1/2 overexpression, with RUNX2 exerting a more pronounced inhibitory effect than RUNX1 (Fig. 2i). To further assess thermogenic function, we employed ERthermac staining, a live-cell thermosensitive dye that reflects intracellular temperature $^{32}$ . Consistent with the observed downregulation of UCP1 and thermogenesis-related pathways in OE-RUNX1 and OE-RUNX2 cells, RUNX1/2 overexpression led to a significant reduction in cellular temperature (Fig. 2j, k). Similarly, JC-1 staining revealed elevated mitochondrial membrane potential, indicative of diminished uncoupled respiration and impaired thermogenic capacity in RUNX1/2-overexpressing cells (Fig. 2l and Supplementary Fig. 4q).

To further dissect this stage-specific effect of RUNX1/2 on thermogenic adipocyte differentiation and function, we employed a TET-ON inducible system to overexpress RUNX1/2 during distinct windows of differentiation: early (day 0-2), mid (day 3-7), and mature adipocytes (day 7-9). RUNX1/2 overexpression during early or mid stages significantly impaired brown adipocyte differentiation gene expression (Supplementary Fig. 5a-o). Notably, induction of RUNX1/2 in mature adipocytes also significantly attenuated thermogenic gene expression (Supplementary Fig. 6a-f). Functionally, RUNX1/2 overexpression at either the early or mature stages inhibited mitochondrial function and cellular respiration (Supplementary Fig. 5p, q and 6g, h). Together, these findings demonstrate that RUNX1/2 not only inhibit brown adipocyte differentiation but also suppress thermogenic reprogramming, thereby acting as negative regulators of both brown adipocyte differentiation and thermogenic capacity.

Conversely, to investigate the functional necessity of RUNX1/2 in thermogenesis, we established RUNX1 knockout (KO-RUNX1), RUNX2 knockout (KO-RUNX2), and double knockout (DKO) brown preadipocyte cell lines using CRISPR/Cas9 technology (Supplementary Fig. 7a-c). Deletion of RUNX1 or RUNX2 led to a significant upregulation of UCP1 and other thermogenic genes, with the effect being most pronounced in the RUNX1/2 double knockout (Fig. 2m and Supplementary Fig. 7d-f). Besides, RUNX1/2 KO modestly enhanced adipogenic differentiation, as assessed by Nile Red staining (Supplementary Fig. 7g-j), though the effect was limited given the near-complete differentiation efficiency in the control condition. Transcriptomic analysis further revealed robust induction of thermogenic gene programs in RUNX1/2 KO adipocytes (Fig. 2n and Supplementary Fig. 7k). Additionally, knockout of either RUNX1 or RUNX2 resulted in an increase in key components of the mitochondrial respiratory complexes, with DKO cells exhibiting the highest levels (Fig. 2o, p). Functionally, deletion of RUNX1/2 resulted in a significant increase in cellular temperature, with DKO cells exhibiting the greatest rise (Fig. 2q, r). This was accompanied by enhanced oxygen consumption rates (OCR) and corroborated by JC-1 staining, indicating improved mitochondrial uncoupling and thermogenic activity (Fig. 2s, t and Supplementary Fig. 7l, m).

# Loss of RUNX1/2 in adipocytes enhances energy expenditure and cold tolerance

To investigate the role of RUNX1/2 in regulating adaptive thermogenesis in vivo, we first utilized an adeno-associated virus (AAV) overexpressing RUNX1/2 in brown adipose tissue (BAT) (Supplementary Fig. 8a, b) and iWAT (Supplementary Fig. 8i, j), respectively. RUNX1/2 OE in BAT significantly reduced UCP1 expression. Functionally, these mice exhibited impaired cold tolerance, as evidenced by a more rapid decline in rectal temperature during acute cold exposure. In addition, indirect calorimetry demonstrated reduced oxygen consumption and energy expenditure following $\beta 3$ -adrenergic stimulation (CL) in RUNX1/2 OE mice (Supplementary Fig. 8c-h). Similar to the BAT results, RUNX1/2 OE in iWAT also led to impaired cold tolerance and reduced oxygen consumption and energy expenditure after $\beta 3$ -adrenergic stimulation. These functional impairments were accompanied by reduced UCP1 expression and diminished adipose browning (Supplementary Fig. 8k-p). In contrast, we utilized AAV-Cre to induce RUNX1 knockdown (RUNX1 KD) in iWAT of RUNX1 flox/flox mice through orthotopic injection (Supplementary Fig. 8q, r). Notably, RUNX1 KD mice maintained their body temperature more effectively than control littermates when exposed to cold $(4^{\circ}C)$ following a two-week acclimatization to thermoneutrality (Supplementary Fig. 8s). Consistent with this observation, thermogenic gene expression levels were markedly upregulated in the iWAT of RUNX1 KD mice, accompanied by enhanced adipocyte browning (Supplementary Fig. 8t-w).

To investigate the essential role of adipocyte-specific RUNX1/2 in adaptive thermogenesis and energy metabolism, we generated adipocyte-specific conditional knockout mice using the Adipoq-Cre driver (RUNX1 AKO and RUNX2 AKO). RUNX1/2 AKO mice and their littermate controls were housed in a thermoneutral environment for two weeks before being subjected to cold exposure. During the initial six hours of cold challenge, both RUNX1 AKO and RUNX2 AKO mice exhibited significantly higher core body temperatures and improved survival rates compared to their control littermates (Fig. 3a-d). Histological analysis revealed reduced adipocyte size and increased UCP1 expression in the iWAT of RUNX1/2 AKO mice, indicating enhanced white adipocyte browning (Fig. 3e-h). Metabolic assessments showed no significant differences between RUNX1/2 AKO and control groups in energy expenditure, food intake, physical activities or respiratory exchange ratio (RER) at thermoneutral condition (Supplementary Fig. 9a-j). However, upon stimulation with CL, RUNX1/2 AKO mice demonstrated enhanced oxygen consumption and carbon dioxide production, suggesting an increase in CL-induced energy expenditure (Fig. 3i-l). Consistently, UCP1 and other key thermogenic and mitochondrial genes were significantly upregulated in RUNX1/2 AKO mice (Fig. 3m-p and Supplementary Fig. 10a-c), further supporting the activation of the browning gene program in iWAT following RUNX1/2 deletion. Interestingly, similar changes were not observed in BAT (Supplementary Fig. 9k, l).

# RUNX1 and RUNX2 differentially regulated diet-induced insulin resistance

We then investigated the potential role of RUNX1/2 in mitigating diet-induced obesity through the enhancement of adaptive thermogenesis. RUNX1/2 AKO and control mice were fed a high-fat diet (HFD) for 12 weeks. Body weight remained comparable between the RUNX1/2 AKO and control groups (Fig. 3q and Supplementary Fig. 10d), and mass of major adipose depots showed no significant differences (Supplementary Fig. 10e). Notably, RUNX1 AKO mice displayed increased food intake, which likely contributed to the similar body weights observed under chronic HFD conditions (Supplementary Fig. 10f). Moreover, due to the limited extent of adipose browning under chronic HFD, no significant changes were detected in energy expenditure and locomotor activity in RUNX1 AKO mice under chronic HFD (Supplementary Fig. 10g-k). Interestingly, RUNX1 AKO mice exhibited enhanced glucose tolerance compared to control mice, suggesting improved systemic glucose handling (Fig. 3r, s). However, insulin sensitivity, assessed after normalization to fasting glucose, was not significantly improved, although we observed reduced circulating insulin level in RUNX1 AKO mice (Fig. 3t and Supplementary Fig. 11a). These findings suggest that RUNX1 and RUNX2 may regulate distinct metabolic pathways in systemic energy homeostasis in response to HFD, although RUNX1 did not affect glucose and insulin metabolism under normal chow condition (Supplementary Fig. 11b, c).

To gain mechanistic insight into the metabolic improvements observed in RUNX1 AKO mice, we performed transcriptomic profiling of iWAT from HFD-fed RUNX1 AKO mice (Fig. 3u). This analysis revealed marked repression of inflammatory pathways, including TNFα, NF-κB, and JAK-STAT signaling. Consistently, the expression of hematopoiesis-related genes was reduced, indicating decreased immune-cell infiltration (Supplementary Fig. 11d, e). In addition, multiple insulin-resistance-associated genes were downregulated. These findings suggest that RUNX1 deficiency improves systemic glucose metabolism not through weight loss, but rather by attenuating adipose inflammation and enhancing insulin-sensitivity pathways, as previously reported ³³,³⁴ . Consistent with this notion, transcriptomic analysis of differentiated brown adipocytes also revealed that RUNX1, but not RUNX2, specifically regulated genes associated with insulin resistance (Supplementary Fig. 11f, g). Furthermore, we observed higher levels of phosphorylated AKT in iWAT of RUNX1 AKO mice compared to control mice (Supplementary Fig. 11h), whereas no differences were detected between RUNX2 AKO and control mice, highlighting a unique role of RUNX1 in modulating insulin signaling and glucose metabolism in the context of diet-induced metabolic stress.

# Deficiency of RUNX1/2 in thermogenic adipocytes improves energy metabolism

To specifically investigate the roles of RUNX1 and RUNX2 in thermogenic adipocytes, we generated thermogenic adipocyte-specific RUNX1 and RUNX2 knockout mice (RUNX1 UKO and RUNX2 UKO) using the Ucp1-Cre driver (Supplementary Fig. 12a-d). Similar to the adipocyte-specific KO model, RUNX1 UKO and RUNX2 UKO mice exhibited enhanced cold tolerance, better survival rates, and higher core body temperatures (Fig. 4a-d) upon cold exposure following a two-week acclimatization to thermoneutrality. Histological analysis revealed a reduction in adipocyte size in iWAT of RUNX1/2 UKO mice compared to controls under cold exposure (Fig. 4e-h). Furthermore, UCP1 expression was significantly upregulated in iWAT from RUNX1/2 UKO mice relative to wild-type littermates following cold challenge. Indirect calorimetry analysis indicated that RUNX1 and RUNX2 UKO mice exhibited minor to no increase in oxygen consumption and carbon dioxide production compared to control mice under thermoneutral conditions (Supplementary Fig. 12e-l). However, upon CL treatment, RUNX1/2 UKO mice displayed a marked augmentation of oxygen consumption and carbon dioxide production compared to control mice (Fig. 4i-l), suggesting that RUNX1/2 deficiency in thermogenic adipocytes enhances β-adrenergic signaling-stimulated thermogenesis.

To elucidate the molecular mechanisms by which RUNX1 and RUNX2 regulate the thermogenic capacity of iWAT, we performed RNA-seq on iWAT samples from control and RUNX1/2 UKO mice under cold exposure (Supplementary Fig. 12m). Gene ontology analysis revealed that the upregulated genes in iWAT of RUNX1/2 UKO mice were significantly enriched in biological processes associated with cellular respiration, oxidative phosphorylation, and other metabolic and energy-producing processes (Fig. 4m, n). Notably, these pathways mirrored the downregulated pathways observed upon RUNX1 and RUNX2 overexpression in differentiated brown adipocytes (Fig. 2b and Supplementary Fig. 4l-o), suggesting a cell-autonomous regulatory network in thermogenic adipocytes. For example, key thermogenic and mitochondrial genes, including Ucp1, Elovl3, and Cidea, were highly upregulated in RUNX1/2 UKO iWAT (Fig. 4o, p), a finding further validated in an independent cohort of mice using RT-qPCR (Fig. 4q, r). Interestingly, similar changes were not observed in BAT of RUNX1/2 KO mice, possibly due to the inherently low expression levels of RUNX1 and RUNX2 in BAT (Supplementary Fig. 12n, o). Additionally, knockout of either RUNX1 or RUNX2 resulted in an increase in key components of the mitochondrial respiratory complexes (Fig. 4s, t and Supplementary Fig. 12p, q), further supporting their roles in metabolic regulation. Collectively, these findings suggest that RUNX1/2 function as thermostatic regulators in thermogenic adipocytes, and their loss enhances thermogenic programming and boosts energy metabolism.

# RUNX1/2 repress thermogenic and adipogenic programs via direct genomic binding

RUNX1 and RUNX2 play critical roles in gene regulation by interacting with various epigenetic modifiers and cofactors $^{35}$ . To further elucidate the molecular mechanisms through which RUNX1/2 regulate thermogenic gene programs, we performed Cleavage Under Targets and Tagmentation (CUT&Tag) sequencing in iWAT tissue. This analysis identified approximately 15,000 and 10,000 genomic binding peaks for RUNX1 and RUNX2, respectively (Supplementary Fig. 13a). De novo motif analysis revealed a strong enrichment of RUNX-binding motifs (Supplementary Fig. 13b, c), confirming the high quality and reliability of the CUT&Tag dataset generated in iWAT. We found that the majority of upregulated genes in RUNX1 and RUNX2 UKO iWAT had nearby RUNX1 or RUNX2 binding sites, respectively (Fig. 5a, b). Importantly, these RUNX1/2-directly regulated genes were significantly enriched in thermogenic pathways, including cellular respiration, fatty acid oxidation, mitochondrial respiratory chain complex activity, and thermogenesis (Fig. 5c, d and Supplementary Fig. 13d). Notably, RUNX1 and RUNX2 directly bound the genomic regions of key thermogenic transcriptional regulators, such as $Ebf2$ and $Pgc1\alpha$, as well as other thermogenic genes (Fig. 5e and Supplementary Fig. 13e). We further validated the direct binding of RUNX1/2 to these thermogenic genes using ChIP-qPCR (Fig. 5f). Collectively, these findings demonstrate that RUNX1 and RUNX2 repress thermogenic gene expression by directly binding to their regulatory genomic regions, thereby acting as key transcriptional repressors of adipocyte thermogenesis.

To determine the functional consequences of RUNX1/2 repression in thermogenic regulation, we examined the expression of EBF2, a key transcriptional regulator of the thermogenic program $^{12}$ . Overexpression of RUNX1 and RUNX2 significantly reduced $Ebf2$ mRNA levels, leading to decreased EBF2 and UCP1 protein expression following seven days of bown adipocyte differentiation (Fig. 5g-i). Conversely, knockout of RUNX1, RUNX2, or both resulted in increased EBF2 mRNA and protein expression, accompanied by UCP1 accumulation (Fig. 5j-l). Notably, EBF2 knockout abolished the increased expression of $Ucp1$ expression in differentiated adipocytes by deletion of RUNX1 or RUNX2 (Fig. 5m), indicating that EBF2 functions downstream of RUNX1 and RUNX2 in thermogenic gene regulation. In addition, to investigate how RUNX1 and RUNX2 inhibit brown adipocyte differentiation, we performed chromatin immunoprecipitation followed by sequencing (ChIP-seq) in brown preadipocytes. Integrative analysis of ChIP-seq and RNA-seq data revealed that 194 genes downregulated upon RUNX1 and RUNX2 overexpression were directly bound by RUNX1 and RUNX2 at their promoter/enhancer regions (Supplementary Fig. 14a). Gene Ontology analysis highlighted significant enrichment in biological processes related to thermogenesis and mitochondrial organization (Supplementary Fig. 14b, c), exemplified by $Ebf2$, $Pgc1a$, $Ucp1$ and $Pparg2$ genomic loci (Supplementary Fig. 14d), suggesting the inhibitory effects of RUNX1/2 on thermogenic and adipogenic gene programs are mediated through direct genomic binding. Consistently, overexpression of RUNX1/2 in preadipocytes markedly reduced the mRNA and protein level of $Pparg2$ (Supplementary Fig. 14e, f), thereby inhibiting downstream adipogenic programs.

Previous studies have reported that members of the RUNX family, particularly RUNX2, exert antagonistic effects on PPAR γ function during mesenchymal stem cell fate decisions ³⁶,³⁷ . We therefore examined whether RUNX1 and RUNX2 inhibit adipogenesis by directly interfering with PPAR γ transcriptional activity. Co-immunoprecipitation assays revealed that both RUNX1 and RUNX2 physically interact with PPAR γ (Supplementary Fig. 14g-i), consistent with previous observations $^{36}$ . However, overexpression of either RUNX1 or RUNX2 did not alter the association of PPAR γ with its canonical co-factors RXR α or EP300 (Supplementary Fig. 14j). Concordantly, RUNX1 or RUNX2 overexpression had no detectable effect on PPAR γ-dependent transcriptional activation of PPAR-responsive elements (PPREs) (Supplementary Fig. 14k). Furthermore, integrative analysis of RUNX1/2 and PPAR γ ChIP-seq datasets failed to reveal significant enrichment of shared target genes or pathways associated with adipocyte metabolism (Supplementary Fig. 14l, m). Collectively, these data suggest that RUNX1 and RUNX2 suppress adipogenic and thermogenic program predominantly through direct transcriptional repression of PPAR γ itself, rather than by impairing the intrinsic transcriptional activity of the PPAR γ protein.

# RUNX1 inhibits thermogenic program through recruiting HDAC1

Next, we sought to identify the cofactors with which RUNX1 and RUNX2 interact to repress the thermogenic program, as RUNX proteins are intrinsically weak transcriptional regulators that rely on cofactors to modulate gene expression $^{38}$ . Previous studies have suggested that RUNX1/2 may function in cooperation with HDAC1 to regulate transcription ³⁹,⁴⁰ . To test this hypothesis, we performed coimmunoprecipitation assays in brown preadipocytes to examine interactions between RUNX1/2 and HDAC1. Our results revealed a strong interaction between RUNX1 and HDAC1, while the interaction between RUNX2 and HDAC1 was weak (Fig. 6a, b). Notably, the RUNX1-HDAC1 interaction appeared to be chromatin-independent, suggesting a potential scaffolding role of RUNX1 in recruiting HDAC1 to target loci (Fig. 6a, b and Supplementary Fig. 15a, b). To further investigate this, we performed HDAC1 CUT&Tag sequencing in iWAT and found extensive overlap between RUNX1/2 and HDAC1 genomic binding sites (Fig. 6c-e). Interestingly, shared binding sites between RUNX1 and HDAC1 were significantly enriched in pathways related to fat cell differentiation, whereas no specific pathways were enriched for the overlapping binding sites between RUNX2 and HDAC1 (Fig. 6f). These findings suggest that RUNX1 and HDAC1 may function together in the genomic regulation of thermogenic genes. Given the cellular complexity of adipose tissue $^{41}$, we further analyzed the binding patterns of RUNX1/2 and HDAC1 specifically in brown adipocytes and observed similar results (Supplementary Fig. 15c-f), reinforcing the notion that RUNX1 preferentially interacts with HDAC1 to modulate thermogenic gene expression.

To functionally test this, we knocked out HDAC1 in RUNX1/2-overexpressing brown preadipocytes (Supplementary Fig. 15g). Interestingly, HDAC1 knockout significantly alleviated the inhibitory effect of RUNX1 overexpression on thermogenic genes but only mildly rescued the repression induced by RUNX2 overexpression in brown adipocytes (Fig. 6g-1). These findings suggest that RUNX1 suppresses thermogenic gene expression primarily by recruiting HDAC1, whereas RUNX2-mediated repression of thermogenic genes appears to be largely independent of HDAC1. To further investigate the mechanism by which RUNX1 represses thermogenic genes through HDAC1, we performed CUT&Tag sequencing for HDAC1 and H3K27ac in differentiated brown adipocytes after overexpression of RUNX1 or RUNX2. Our analysis revealed that RUNX1 overexpression promoted HDAC1 binding and led to reduced H3K27ac modification at the regulatory genomic regions of key thermogenic genes, including Ucp1, Ebf2, and $Pgc1\alpha$ (Fig. 6m). In contrast, RUNX2 overexpression had little effect on HDAC1 recruitment or H3K27ac levels near these genes (Supplementary Fig. 15h). These results indicate that RUNX1 represses thermogenic gene expression through HDAC1-mediated epigenetic remodeling, whereas RUNX2 likely employs a different mechanism to regulate thermogenic programs.

# RUNX2 regulates thermogenic gene program through phase separation

Previous studies have demonstrated that RUNX2 can undergo phase separation to regulate various biological processes, including cleidocranial dysplasia and osteoblast differentiation ⁴²,⁴³ . To test whether RUNX1/2 phase separation regulates thermogenic adipocyte differentiation and function, we first identified extensive intrinsically disordered regions (IDRs) within mouse RUNX1 and RUNX2 (Fig. 7a and Supplementary Fig. 16a), suggesting their potential for phase separation. Expression of GFP-tagged RUNX1 and RUNX2 in U2OS cells revealed that both proteins formed phase-separated condensates (Fig. 7b and Supplementary Fig. 16b). However, RUNX1 predominantly formed gel-like condensates with limited fluorescence recovery after photobleaching (FRAP), whereas RUNX2 formed dynamic, liquid-like condensates exhibiting rapid fluorescence recovery (Fig. 7c, d and Supplementary Fig. 16c, d). Immunostaining of endogenous proteins in brown preadipocytes confirmed prominent phase separation exclusively for RUNX2 (Fig. 7e).

To further investigate the functional significance of RUNX2 IDR-driven phase separation in brown adipogenesis, we generated multiple RUNX2 mutants. A mutant lacking the IDR ( δ IDR) failed to form condensates, whereas reintroduction of an IDR derived from the FUS protein (FUS-IDR) restored its phase separation capability (Fig. 7f, g). Notably, the RUNX2 δ IDR mutant retained normal genomic DNA-binding activity but exhibited markedly diminished transcriptional activation capacity (Supplementary Fig. 16e-h). Functionally, when these RUNX2 mutants were introduced into RUNX2-knockout brown preadipocytes followed by differentiation induction, the δ IDR mutant lost its ability to repress thermogenic gene expression and mitochondrial energy metabolism (Fig. 7h-l). In contrast, the RUNX2-FUS-IDR mutant robustly suppressed thermogenic gene expression and mitochondrial bioenergetics (Fig. 7h-l).

To further delineate the mechanistic basis of RUNX2 condensates-mediated thermogenic repression, we examined their interaction with thermogenic co-activators and repressive chromatin modifiers. Specifically, we tested whether RUNX2 condensates sequester transcriptional co-activators (EP300, HDAC3, the known thermogenic activators that interact with RUNX2) ¹⁸,⁴⁴ or recruit repressive factors (HDAC2, TLE1, the known RUNX2-interacted co-repressors) $^{45}$ . Interestingly, EP300, HDAC3, HDAC2, and TLE1 alone failed to form condensates, yet RUNX2 overexpression facilitated their incorporation into RUNX2 condensates (Fig. 7m and Supplementary Fig.16i-1), whereas RUNX2- δ IDR lacked this ability (Supplementary Fig. 16m). In particular, EP300 was sequestered within RUNX2 condensates, as demonstrated by co-localization analysis (Fig. 7m), which likely attenuates its transcriptional co-activator function. Supporting this model, CUT&Tag analysis revealed significantly altered binding efficiency of HDAC2 at thermogenic gene promoters between RUNX2 wild-type and RUNX2 δ IDR adipocytes, whereas the genomic binding profile of EP300 was less extensively remodeled, possibly reflecting partial retention of EP300 at its target loci despite sequestration into RUNX2 condensates (Fig. 7n and Supplementary Fig. 16n, o). Together, these results suggest that the phase separation property of RUNX2 condensates directly modulate thermogenic gene transcription (Supplementary Fig. 17a-c) by sequestering EP300 and recruiting repressive factors such as HDAC2.

# Pharmacological inhibition of RUNX1/2 enhance adipose thermogenesis and energy metabolism

Given the repressive roles of RUNX1 and RUNX2 in adaptive thermogenesis and energy metabolism, we next explored their potential as therapeutic targets using pharmacological inhibitors. We first demonstrated that pharmacological inhibition of RUNX1 and RUNX2 using small-molecule inhibitors RO5-3335 and CADD522 significantly enhanced the expression levels of UCP1 mRNA and protein in differentiated brown adipocytes ⁴⁶,⁴⁷ (Fig. 8a, b). Meanwhile, OCR analysis exhibited an improved mitochondrial function after the treatment of either inhibitors (Fig. 8c, d). Subsequently, mice were treated with RO5-3335 or CADD522 for one week, after which we evaluated adipose tissue thermogenesis and systemic energy metabolism. Notably, mice treated with either inhibitor exhibited significantly higher core body temperatures and improved survival rates compared to their control littermates under cold exposure (Fig. 8e-h). Treatment with RUNX1/2 inhibitors elevated the expression of key thermogenic genes, including Ucp1, Pgc1a, and Ebf2 (Fig. 8i-1 and Supplementary Fig. 18a, b). Histological analysis further revealed reduced adipocyte size and increased UCP1 expression in the inguinal white adipose tissue, indicating enhanced white adipocyte browning (Fig. 8m, n and Supplementary Fig. 18c, d). Collectively, these findings highlight RUNX1 and RUNX2 as promising therapeutic targets to enhance adipocyte thermogenic capacity and combat obesity-related metabolic disorders.

# Discussion

RUNX proteins have long been recognized as critical regulators of cellular differentiation. RUNX2 is essential for osteogenic differentiation and skeletal development, whereas RUNX1 plays pivotal role in hematopoietic lineage commitment at distinct developmental stages $^{20}$ . However, the involvement of RUNX proteins in determining thermogenic adipocyte fate remains poorly understood. In this study, we identified RUNX1 and RUNX2 as significant transcriptional repressors of thermogenic adipocyte activation. Expression of RUNX1/2 was dynamically reduced in response to thermal stress and exhibited positive correlations with obesity and adverse metabolic phenotypes. Adipocyte-specific genetic deletion of RUNX1 or RUNX2 promoted beige fat formation, although their effects on systemic metabolism differed, with RUNX1 deletion uniquely enhancing glucose metabolism. Importantly, pharmacological inhibition of RUNX1/2 stimulated adipose thermogenesis and improved systemic energy metabolism. Our results reveal previously unrecognized roles for RUNX proteins in adipocyte browning (Fig. 8o), underscoring their potential as promising therapeutic targets for treating metabolic disorders. Emerging evidence implicates RUNX1 as critical modulators of thermogenesis and energy metabolism, although its precise role remains incompletely understood. One study demonstrated that erythropoietin promotes energy metabolism by inhibiting lipogenic gene programs through RUNX1-mediated transcriptional regulation $^{48}$ . Another report showed that CDK6 kinase activity negatively regulates adipocyte browning via suppression of RUNX1, with RUNX1 positively regulating Ucp1 expression by directly binding its proximal promoter region under CDK6-deficient conditions $^{22}$ . However, in the same study, deletion of RUNX1 in wildtype mice induced the expression of thermogenic genes, suggesting the controversial roles of RUNX1 in regulating adipose browning. In our study, we systematically examined the function of RUNX1 on adaptive thermogenesis using transient modulation (overexpression or knockdown via adeno-associated virus), as well as adipocyte-specific and thermogenic adipocyte-specific RUNX1 knockout mouse models. We consistently identified RUNX1 as a suppressor of adaptive thermogenesis both in vitro and in vivo, reinforcing its role as a transcriptional barrier to adipocyte browning. RUNX1 and RUNX2 share structural similarities but exhibit distinct expression patterns, regulatory mechanisms, and biological functions<sup>35</sup>. They function in complex with their obligate partner, corebinding factor CBF β, to regulate gene expression $^{49}$ . Notably, RUNX genes display paradoxical roles, acting as both transcriptional activators and repressors depending on the cellular context $^{50}$ . Here, we found that deletion of RUNX1 or RUNX2 both promoted thermogenic gene program, with the double deletion of RUNX1 and RUNX2 showing the most robust effect, suggesting the redundant regulatory mechanisms of RUNX1 and RUNX2 on thermogenic gene program. Interestingly, although RUNX1/2 AKO mice displayed $\sim 5$ -fold higher Ucp1 expression in iWAT upon cold exposure, this increase was insufficient to confer measurable improvements in whole-body energy expenditure under chronic HFD feeding, where adipose thermogenesis is typically suppressed. This finding aligns with prior reports that Ucp1 upregulation does not always translate into reduced adiposity under long-term HFD due to compensatory metabolic adaptations ⁵¹,⁵²,⁵³ . Consistent with this notion, RUNX1/2 AKO mice exhibited energy expenditure comparable to that of control mice under chronic HFD conditions. Notably, RUNX1 AKO mice showed improved appetite, which may further compromise the energy expenditure, although the underlying mechanism remains unclear and warrants further investigation. Remarkably, only RUNX1 knockout improved glucose tolerance in response to HFD, indicating distinct regulatory roles of RUNX1 and RUNX2 on glucose and insulin metabolism gene program. We propose that RUNX1 deficiency enhances systemic glucose control not through weight loss but by mitigating adipose inflammation and activating insulin-sensitizing pathways, as supported by prior reports ³³,³⁴ . Interestingly, the effect of RUNX1 on systemic glucose metabolism appears to be organ-specific, as skeletal muscle RUNX1 expression was lower in obese individuals compared with normal-weight individuals and was positively correlated with insulin sensitivity $^{54}$ . Due to diverse protein-protein and protein-chromatin interactions, RUNX1/2 modulate chromatin states through multiple context-dependent mechanisms $^{55}$ . Previous studies have shown that both the Runt and transactivation (TAD) domains of RUNX proteins physically interact with HDACs $^{50}$ . In our study, we found that RUNX1 suppressed the thermogenic gene program by recruiting HDAC1, leading to reduced H3K27 acetylation and decreased chromatin accessibility, whereas RUNX2 exhibited a much weaker interaction with HDAC1 and HDAC1 deletion failed to rescue the downregulation of thermogenic genes upon RUNX2 overexpression. These results demonstrated that, although RUNX1 and RUNX2 share similar protein sequences, particularly at the C-terminus, which serves as a structural platform for various protein interactions $^{38}$, they differ in their interaction with HDAC1 in the context of brown adipogenesis. Emerging evidence indicates that liquid-liquid phase separation (LLPS) provides a novel mechanism for transcriptional regulation in metabolism ⁵⁶,⁵⁷ . Recent study has revealed that thioesterase superfamily member 1 (Them1) forms metabolically active puncta near lipid droplets and mitochondria upon β-adrenergic stimulation, thereby reducing fatty acid availability and limiting thermogenesis $^{58}$ . Here, we demonstrated that RUNX2 formed phase-separated condensates in U2OS cells and brown preadipocytes. Disruption of RUNX2 LLPS by deletion of its IDR impaired its function in brown adipocyte differentiation, whereas fusion of the FUS IDR to RUNX2 $^{\Delta \mathrm{IDR}}$ restored its activity in this process. Interestingly, although RUNX1 also contains classical IDR, it preferentially formed gel-like condensates in U2OS cells and showed limited phase separation in brown preadipocytes. The distinct phase separation properties of RUNX1 and RUNX2 likely arise from differences in their interacting partners, and the underlying mechanisms remain to be elucidated. Previous studies have reported bidirectional and autoregulatory interactions between RUNX1 and RUNX2 ⁵⁹,⁶⁰, indicating that these transcription factors may form interconnected regulatory circuits. Consistent with this, our observations suggest that RUNX2 condensates can influence RUNX1 expression, and that combined deletion of RUNX1 and RUNX2 leads to additive enhancement of UCP1 expression, supporting a cooperative role in suppressing thermogenic programs. Notably, we observed that the functional output of RUNX2 is context-dependent and influenced by the presence of RUNX1. One possible explanation is that RUNX2 overexpression activates compensatory or feedback mechanisms involving RUNX1 or shared transcriptional cofactors, thereby modulating the net thermogenic response. Alternatively, loss of RUNX1 may reshape the chromatin landscape or broader transcriptional network in a manner that limits the ability of RUNX2 to regulate thermogenic gene expression. In this scenario, RUNX2 may require a permissive chromatin or cofactor environment established by RUNX1 to exert its full regulatory capacity.

Although we demonstrated a critical role for RUNX1/2 in brown adipocyte differentiation in vitro, enhanced thermogenic adipocyte activation was observed only in the iWAT of RUNX1/2 AKO and UKO mice, with no induction of thermogenic genes in BAT compared to controls. This is likely due to the low expression of RUNX1/2 in mature BAT adipocytes, where Adipoq-Cre and Ucp1-Cre are highly active. Deleting RUNX1/2 in preadipocytes using PDGFRα-Cre might clarify their role in BAT physiology. However, given the essential function of RUNX1/2 in PDGFRα-expressing mesenchymal cells ⁶¹,⁶², viable preadipocyte-specific RUNX1/2 knockout mice could not be generated. As an alternative approach, we performed AAV-mediated RUNX1/2 overexpression in BAT, which resulted in impaired cold tolerance and reduced whole-body energy expenditure, supporting a suppressive role of RUNX1/2 in thermogenic BAT function. Altered BAT activity likely also contributed indirectly to iWAT beijing. Interestingly, RUNX1/2 AKO mice exhibited a greater increase in energy expenditure than UKO mice following CL316,243 treatment. This difference may reflect the universal and high recombination efficiency of the Adipoq-Cre driver, resulting in RUNX1/2 deletion in both Ucp1+ and Ucp1- adipocytes in AKO, but not UKO, mice. In addition to the cell-autonomous effects of RUNX1/2 in thermogenic adipocytes, the loss of RUNX1/2 in Ucp1- adipocytes may also contribute to the more pronounced thermogenic phenotype in AKO mice. RUNX proteins undergo posttranslational modifications that significantly impact their cellular localization, stability, and DNA-binding affinity $^{20}$ . For example, erythropoietin treatment reduces the expression of the WAT ubiquitin ligase FBXW7, thereby increasing RUNX1 stability and promoting energy metabolism $^{48}$ . Additionally, CDK6 interacts with RUNX1 and phosphorylates it on serine 303, and deletion of CDK6 leads to reduced RUNX1 phosphorylation and prevents its degradation $^{22}$ . In our study, we revealed a distinct depot-specific expression pattern of RUNX1/2 proteins across adipose tissues, yet the upstream regulatory signals controlling these differences remain unclear. Notably, RUNX1 displayed opposing regulation between iWAT and eWAT in response to HFD, suggesting that it may be governed by nutritional stress-specific regulatory cues or depot-intrinsic signaling pathways. Future work is needed to define the upstream signaling networks and specific modifications that govern RUNX1/2 abundance and activity under nutritional challenges. Moreover, our findings indicate that RUNX1 exhibits a stronger response to obesity signals than RUNX2 in both HFD-induced obesity models and obese individuals, underscoring a potentially critical role for RUNX1 in regulating energy metabolism under obese conditions.

Although RUNX1 and RUNX2 display broadly similar expression patterns during adipocyte development and differentiation, their transcriptional activities and downstream functions may diverge, which warrants further investigation. We acknowledge potential technical limitations in the human scRNA-seq analysis. RUNX2 expression is generally lower and more sparsely detected than RUNX1 in adipocyte-lineage cells, which may reduce statistical power to detect BMI-dependent associations. Thus, while the absence of a BMI correlation for RUNX2 aligns with our mouse functional data, we cannot fully exclude contributions from detection sensitivity or sampling limitations. Notably, RUNX1 and RUNX2 display comparable expression patterns between male and female mice and in human datasets, suggesting a conserved regulatory mechanism across sexes. It will also be of interest to further explore the regulation of RUNX1 and RUNX2 during short-term HFD exposure and to directly assess their association with diet-induced thermogenesis (DIT) in future studies. Although RUNX1 AKO mice exhibited several metabolic improvements under HFD, we did not observe a corresponding long-term divergence in body weight, a phenotype typically associated with sustained DIT. Given the partially redundant roles of RUNX1 and RUNX2 in adipose thermogenesis, future studies employing RUNX1/ RUNX2 double-knockout models will be required to more definitively evaluate their contribution to DIT-associated metabolic effects. In sum, our study demonstrates that deletion of RUNX1 and RUNX2 promotes the browning of white adipose tissue via divergent mechanisms, either through HDAC1-mediated epigenetic remodeling or condensate-dependent transcriptional regulation. Moreover, the distinct ways in which RUNX1 and RUNX2 regulate thermogenesis, along with their differential effects on glucose and insulin metabolism, lay the groundwork for developing precision therapeutic strategies to ameliorate obesity and metabolic syndromes.

# Methods

# Animal studies

All animal experiments were conducted in compliance with protocols approved by the Ethics Committee for Animal Experiments at Guangzhou Laboratory (GZLAB-AUCP-2022-12-A04). Wild-type (WT) male C57BL/6J mice were obtained from GemPharmatech (Guangzhou, China). Runx1-Flox mice were purchased from Shanghai Model Organisms Center, Inc. Runx2-Flox mice (C57BL/6J, S-CKO-01564), Adipoq-Cre (C57BL/6N, C001042), and Ucp1-Cre (C57BL/6N, C001259) mice were purchased from Cyagen (Suzhou, China). Mice were housed under standard laboratory conditions with a 12-hour light/dark cycle (lights on at 7:00, off at 19:00), maintained at an ambient temperature of $22^{\circ}C$ with 60% relative humidity unless otherwise indicated, and provided ad libitum access to standard chow and water. Mice were fed a normal chow diet (1016706476803973120, Keaoxieli Feed, Beijing, China) containing 6.5% fat, 19.5% protein and no cholesterol, unless subjected to a high-fat diet (HFD) intervention. For HFD intervention, male mice aged 7 weeks were maintained on a high-fat diet (Research Diet, D12492) containing 60% kcal from fat, 20% kcal from protein, and 20% kcal from carbohydrates, with a metabolizable energy density of $5.21\mathrm{kcal / g}$ for 12 weeks. Body weight measurements were taken every two weeks to monitor the progression of weight gain.

To pharmacologically inhibit RUNX1 or RUNX2 activity, mice received intraperitoneal injections of the RUNX1 inhibitor Ro5-3335 (15 mg/kg, T4687, TargetMol) or the RUNX2 inhibitor CADD522 (10 mg/kg, T8334, TargetMol) for seven consecutive days. For induction of beige adipocytes via $\beta 3$ -adrenergic stimulation, CL-316,243 (1 mg/kg, HY-116771A, MCE) was administered intraperitoneally once daily for seven consecutive days.

# Cold tolerance test

Mice were singly housed in climate-controlled rodent incubators (Sable Systems). maintained for 2 weeks at $30^{\circ}C$ with free access to food and water. Then, mice were placed in prechilled cages at $4^{\circ}C$ with bedding, free access to standard chow and water, and the cage lid partly open. Rectal temperatures were recorded hourly using a thermometer (Suzhou snsek, FT3400) to assess thermogenic capacity. Individual mice were removed from the study and euthanized if core body temperature fell $\geq 10^{\circ}C$ from baseline measurement.

# Glucose tolerance test (GTT) and Insulin tolerance test (ITT)

For the glucose tolerance test, mice were fasted overnight before receiving an intraperitoneal injection of a glucose solution (1.5 g/kg for mice under normal chow condition; 0.75 g/kg for mice under HFD condition). For the insulin tolerance test, mice were fasted for 6 hours prior to an intraperitoneal injection of insulin (1.25 U/kg body weight). Blood glucose levels were measured over a period of indicated time using a glucometer (Bayer).

# Metabolic cage analysis

Whole-body metabolic rates were measured using the Promethion Metabolic Cage System (Sable Systems). RUNX1/2 AKO or UKO mice and their littermate controls were acclimated to $30^{\circ}C$ for 2 weeks prior to their transfer to the metabolic cages. RUNX1/2 OE in BAT and iWAT mice and their controls were maintained at an ambient temperature of $22^{\circ}C$ for metabolic cage analysis. During the assessment, mice received a single intraperitoneal injection of CL-316,243 at a dose of $0.1\mathrm{mg/kg}$ body weight to stimulate thermogenic activity.

# Cell culture

Immortalized primary Ucp1-2A-GFP brown pre-adipocytes (a kind gift from Dr. Qiurong Ding, Laboratory of Stem Cell and Liver Disease, Shanghai Institute of Nutrition and Health, CAS) were cultured to confluence in DMEM supplemented with 20% FBS and 1% penicillin-streptomycin[24]. Differentiation was initiated by incubating these cells in an induction medium consisting of DMEM, 10% FBS, 1 μg/mL insulin, $1\mathrm{nM}$ triiodothyronine (T3, Sigma-Aldrich, T2877), $125\mathrm{nM}$ indomethacin (Sigma-Aldrich, I8280), $0.5\mathrm{mM}$ IBMX, $1\mu M$ rosiglitazone, and $1\mu M$ Dex for 3 days. Subsequently, the medium was exchanged for DMEM with 10% FBS, 1 μg/mL insulin, and $1\mathrm{nM}$ T3, and the cells were cultured for an additional 4 days. To evaluate cellular thermogenesis, fully differentiated adipocytes were incubated with $250~\mathrm{nM}$ ERthermac dye (EMD Millipore, SCT057) for 30 minutes before analysis by flow cytometry using an Agilent Novocyte Advanteon instrument. Stromal vascular fraction (SVF) cells were isolated from inguinal white adipose tissue (iWAT) for beige adipocyte induction. SVF cells were cultured in Dulbecco's Modified Eagle's Medium (DMEM; Gibco, C11965500BT) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS; Lonsera, S712-012S) and 1% penicillin-streptomycin (Gibco, 15070063) until they reached confluence. For beige adipogenesis, the cells were incubated with differentiation medium composed of DMEM with 10% FBS, $1\mu M$ dexamethasone (Dex; Sigma-Aldrich, D4902), $0.5\mathrm{mM}$ 3-isobutyl-1-methylxanthine (IBMX; Sigma-Aldrich, I5879), $1\mu M$ rosiglitazone (Sigma-Aldrich, R2408), and $1\mu \mathrm{g / mL}$ insulin (Sigma-Aldrich, I9278) for 3 days. Thereafter, the medium was replaced with DMEM containing 10% FBS and $1\mu \mathrm{g / mL}$ insulin, and cells were maintained for an additional 4 days to complete differentiation.

# Plasmid construction for gene knockout and overexpression

For gene knockout experiments, specific guide RNAs (sgRNAs) targeting mouse Runx1, Runx2, and Hdac1 were individually cloned into the lentiCRISPR v2 plasmid. For overexpression studies, HA-tagged, codon-optimized mouse Runx1 and Runx2 cDNAs were synthesized by Guangzhou IGE Biotechnology Co., Ltd. and subcloned into the pLVX-Hygromycin and pLVX-TetOne-Puromycin vectors, respectively. RUNX2 mutants were generated by PCR amplification from RUNX2 cDNA and inserted into the pcDNA3 and pLVX-Hygromycin vectors. In addition, mCherry-tagged, codon-optimized mouse Hdac2, Hdac3, Ep300, Ebf2, and Tle1 cDNAs were synthesized by Guangzhou IGE Biotechnology and cloned into the pEF1A-puromycin vector. Primer sequences used for cloning are provided in Table S1.

# Lentiviral production and Generation of stable cell lines

293T cells were transfected with the lentiviral plasmid alongside packaging plasmids (psPAX2 and pMD2.G) using polyethyleneimine (PEI) transfection reagent. Viral supernatants were harvested at 48h and 72h post-transfection and subsequently filtered through a $0.45\mu m$ filter to remove cellular debris. Brown preadipocytes were infected with the prepared lentivirus, and stable cell lines were established by selecting transduced cells with the appropriate antibiotics: $250~\mu \mathrm{g / mL}$ hygromycin B (Catalog No. S2908, Selleck), $2.5~\mu \mathrm{g / mL}$ puromycin (S7417, Selleck), or $350~\mu \mathrm{g / mL}$ blasticidin (S7419, Selleck).

# RNA isolation, cDNA synthesis, and quantitative PCR.

Total RNA was extracted from cells or tissues using RNA Reagent (Aidlab, RN2801) following the manufacturer's protocol. For reverse transcription, 1 μg of RNA was converted to cDNA using the Transcriptor First Strand cDNA Synthesis Kit (Vazyme, R323-01) as per the manufacturer's guidelines. Quantitative PCR (qPCR) was subsequently conducted on an QuantStudio 5 instrument (Applied Biosystems) using SYBR-Green PCR Master Mix (Vazyme, Q711-03) to amplify and quantify target mRNA levels. The expression level of Arbp was used for normalization in mouse samples. Primer sequences are provided in Supplementary Table S2.

# Western blot

Cells and tissues were lysed in RIPA buffer (Beyotime, P0013B) supplemented with PMSF (Beyotime, ST507) and a protease inhibitor cocktail (Selleck, B14002). Protein extracts were separated on SDS-PAGE, transferred onto nitrocellulose membrane, and blotted with the indicated primary antibodies including rabbit anti-UCP1 (Abcam, ab10983), rabbit antiRUNX1 (Proteintech, 25315-1-AP), rabbit anti-RUNX2 (CST, 8486S), anti-HSP90 (Abmart, T55548M), rabbit anti-AKT (CST, 9272S) and rabbit anti-p-AKT (S473) (CST, 4060S), mouse anti-HDAC1 (Abcam, ab7028), , mouse anti-VINCULIN (CST, 18799S) and mouse anti-OXPHOS (Abcam, ab110413), rabbit anti-CD45 (Abmart, PA6232S), rabbit anti-Adiponectin (Abcam, ab22554), mouse anti- PPARγ (Santa Cruz, sc-7273), rabbit anti-P300 (Abcam, ab275378), rabbit anti-HDAC2 (Abcam, ab32117), rabbit anti-β3-Tubulin (CST, #5568), rabbit anti-Beta Actin (Proteintech, 81115-1-RR), rabbit anti-RXRα (Abmart, T58640S). Membranes were then incubated with the corresponding HRP-conjugated secondary antibodies (anti-rabbit IgG, CST, 7074S; anti-mouse IgG, CST, 926-32210) for signal detection. Images were captured using a ChemiDoc imaging system (BioRad) under standard acquisition parameters. All unprocessed scans of the blots are provided in the Source Data.

# Oxygen Consumption Rate (OCR) assays

OCR was measured using the Seahorse XFe Extracellular Flux Analyzer (Agilent) in a 96-well plate. In brief, differentiated brown adipocytes were cultured for 7 days before analysis, cells were sequentially treated with $1 \mu M$ oligomycin (MCE, HY-N6782), $1.5 \mu M$ carbonyl cyanide-p trifluoromethoxyphenylhydrazone (FCCP, Sigma, C2920), $0.5\mu M$ antimycin A (Sigma, A8674), and 0.5 $\mu M$ rotenone (Sigma, R8875) to assess uncoupled respiration. OCR values were normalized to total cellular protein content.

# Tissue histology and fluorescence imaging

Adipose tissue was isolated from mice, fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde, and embedded in paraffin blocks. The blocks were sectioned at $10~{\mum}$ thickness and stained with H&E following the standard protocol. For Ucp1 staining, tissue slides were deparaffinized, rehydrated, and incubated with rabbit anti-UCP1 (Abcam, ab10983), followed by visualization using DAB staining (SK-4105, Vector Laboratories). Images were acquired using a digital slide scanner (Axio Scan Z1, Carl Zeiss).

# Immunofluorescence

For immunofluorescence staining of cultured cells, cells were seeded onto 8-well chamber slides, fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde at room temperature, permeabilized with 0.3% Triton X-100 in PBS, and blocked with 5% bovine serum albumin (BSA) in PBS. Cells were then incubated with primary antibodies against $\mathrm{PPAR}\gamma$ diluted in blocking buffer at $4^{\circ}C$ overnight, followed by incubation with appropriate fluorophore-conjugated secondary antibodies at room temperature for 1 h . Nuclei were counterstained with DAPI (Cell Signaling Technology, 4083).

For immunofluorescence staining of tissue cryosections, adipose tissues were fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde at $4^{\circ}C$ overnight, cryoprotected in 30% sucrose overnight, embedded in OCT compound, and sectioned at $10\mu m$ thickness using a cryostat. Sections were mounted onto glass slides, permeabilized with 0.3% Triton X-100 in PBS, and blocked with 5% BSA for 1 h at room temperature. Sections were then incubated with primary antibodies against UCP1 (Abcam, ab10983), PPARγ (Santa Cruz Biotechnology, sc-7273), and PDGFRα (R&D Systems, AF-307-NA) at $4^{\circ}C$ overnight. After washing, sections were incubated with appropriate fluorophore-conjugated secondary antibodies for 1 h at room temperature and counterstained with DAPI (Cell Signaling Technology, 4083). Images were acquired using a Zeiss LSM 800 confocal microscope.

# Local delivery of adeno-associated virus (AAV) in adipose tissues

Local delivery of AAV in adipose tissues was performed as previously described[63]. In brief, Adeno-associated virus (AAV) vector-mediated overexpression of mouse RUNX1, control (ZsGreen), Adeno-associated virus (AAV)-Cre-ZsGreen and Control (ZsGreen) were purchased from Hanbio biotechnology (Shanghai, China). A total of $50~\mu l$ of $1^{*}10^{13}\mathrm{Vg / ml}$ of each AAV diluted in PBS was injected into the inguinal fat pads and brown fat pads of mice.

# Nile red staining

Cells were washed once with PBS, fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde for 15 min and stained with $0.5~\mu M$ Nile red solution (Sigma, N3013) and 1 ng/mL DAPI (CST, 4083) for 10 minutes at ambient temperature. After staining, cells were washed three times with PBS and imaged using an EVOS imaging system (Thermo Fisher).

# Oil Red O staining

Differentiated adipocytes were washed three with PBS, fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde at room temperature for 15 min and washed twice again with PBS. Cells were then stained with Modified Oil Red O Stain Kit (Solarbio, G1261). Next, cells were washed three times with tap water and left to dry at room temperature. For visualizing, Olympus IX7 was used.

# Triglyceride assay

Cellular triglyceride content was quantified using the Triglyceride (TG) Content Assay Kit (Sangon Biotech, D799795) according to the manufacturer's instructions. Briefly, cells were washed twice with cold PBS and lysed in the provided extraction buffer. Lysates were centrifuged at $12,000 \times g$ for $10 \mathrm{~min}$ at $4^{\circ} C$, and the supernatants were collected for subsequent analysis. Triglycerides were enzymatically hydrolyzed to glycerol and quantified by colorimetric detection. Absorbance was measured at $510 \mathrm{~nm}$ using a microplate reader. TG concentrations were calculated from a standard curve and normalized to total protein content.

# Mouse insulin ELISA

Serum insulin concentrations were measured using a commercial mouse Insulin ELISA Kit (Abmart, AB-B215511) according to the manufacturer's instructions. Briefly, blood samples were centrifuged to obtain serum, which was subsequently analyzed by ELISA. Absorbance was measured at $450\mathrm{nm}$ using a microplate reader, and insulin concentrations were calculated from a standard curve.

# Dual-luciferase reporter assay

The synthetic construct containing four tandem copies of the RUNX2 binding motif (AACCACAGACGATAACCACAG ACGATAACCACAGACGATAACCACAG) was cloned into the M50 Super $8\times$ TOPFlash vector between the KpnI and Ascl restriction sites, generating RUNX2-motif-luc plasmid.

To test the transcriptional activity of RUNX2 or RUNX2 $^{\Delta \mathrm{IDR}}$ on the cognate motif, 293T cells were co-transfected with RUNX2-motif-luc, Renilla plasmid, and the indicated RUNX2 constructs (vector, WT, or ΔIDR). Dual-luciferase activity was measured using the Dual-Luciferase® Reporter Assay System (Promega, Cat. #E1910) following the manufacturer's instructions, and luminescence was detected with a BioTek SYNERGY NEO2 luminometer. PPAR γ transcriptional activity was assessed using a PPAR-responsive element (PPRE)-driven luciferase reporter assay. Cells were co-transfected with a PPRE-luciferase reporter construct, a Renilla luciferase control plasmid, and expression vectors encoding RUNX1, RUNX2, or empty vector, with the total amount of transfected DNA kept constant. Dual-luciferase activity was measured 24 h posttransfection using a dual-luciferase reporter assay system. PPRE-driven firefly luciferase activity was normalized to Renilla luciferase activity and expressed relative to the empty vector control.

# Electron microscopy

Differentiated brown adipocytes were fixed for $1 \mathrm{~h}$ at room temperature with fixative solution 2.5% glutaraldehyde, and then treated with 1% osmiumtetroxide (OsO4)/ 1.5% potassium ferrocyanide (KFeCN6) for 1 h. Subsequent dehydration in grades of alcohol (10 min each at 50% , 70% , 80% and 90% , and twice for 10 min at 100% ). After dehydration, propyleneoxide was added to the dish and the cells were lifted off using a transfer pipet, pelleted and infiltrated overnight in a 1:1 mixture of propyleneoxide and TAAB Epon (TAABLaboratories Equipment). The samples were then embedded in TAAB Epon and polymerized at $60^{\circ}C$ for 48 h. Ultrathin sections (about $70~\mathrm{nm}$ ) were cut on the Reichert Ultracut-S microtome, picked up onto copper grids stained with lead citrate and examined on the JEOL 1200EX transmission electron microscope or the TecnaiG2 Spirit BioTWIN system and images were recorded with the AMT 2k CCD camera.

# Co-immunoprecipitation

Nuclear fractions were isolated from brown pre-adipocytes using a nuclei isolation kit (Sigma-Aldrich, NUC101) according to the manufacturer's instructions. Briefly, nuclear pellets were resuspended in 300 μL lysis buffer (50 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.5,150 mM KCl, 1 mM EDTA, 0.5% NP-40) and incubated at $4^{\circ}C$ for $30\mathrm{min}$ . Lysates were centrifuged at $13,000~\mathrm{rpm}$ for $20\mathrm{min}$ at $4^{\circ}C$, and the supernatants were collected as nuclear extracts. For each immunoprecipitation, nuclear extract was incubated with 4 μg of the indicated antibody and 30 μL Dynabeads Protein A (Thermo Fisher Scientific, 10002D) in coimmunoprecipitation buffer (50 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.5,150 mM KCl, 1 mM EDTA, 0.5% NP-40) in a total reaction volume of 500 μL . Immunoprecipitation reactions were incubated at $4^{\circ}C$ overnight with gentle rotation. Beads were then washed three times with co-immunoprecipitation buffer and once with PBS containing 0.05% NP-40. Bound proteins were eluted and analyzed by western blotting.

# RNA-seq and analyses

Total RNAs were extracted as described above. RNA libraries were constructed with Fast RNA-seq Lib Prep Kit V2 (Cat. No. RK2030) and sequenced using Illumina Novaseq X PLUS. Raw sequencing reads were processed by fastp with parameter "-q 20 --detect_adapter_for_pe --trim.poly_g" to trim the adapter and poly G end. Then, trimmed reads were mapped to mouse reference genome (mm39) by STAR in 2-pass mode. featureCounts were used to count reads of gene after mapping $^{64}$ . Read counts were normalized to counts per million (CPM) to create a matrix of normalized expression values. We used DESeq $^{265}$ to determine differentially expressed genes with threshold q-value < 0.01 and $\left|\mathrm{FC}\right| \geq 1.5$ . GO, KEGG enrichment analysis and GSEA analysis were performed by clusterProfiler $^{66}$ .

# ChIP assays, Cut&tag assays and analyses

RUNX1 and RUNX2 ChIP was performed as described $^{67}$ . In brief, brown preadipocytes were fixed with 1% formaldehyde for $10\mathrm{min}$ and the reaction was quenched by $0.125M$ glycine. Cells were then washed with PBS for two times. Cells were lysed in ChIP buffer ( 1% SDS, $50\mathrm{mM}$ Tris-HCl, pH 8.0, $10\mathrm{mM}$ EDTA, and protease inhibitor cocktail) for $30\mathrm{min}$ at $4^{\circ}C$ . The DNA was fragmented to 200-500bp by sonication and centrifuged at $12,000g$ for $5\mathrm{min}$ . The supernatant was diluted with ChIP IP buffer ( $50\mathrm{mM}$ Tris-HCl, pH 8.0, $150\mathrm{mM}$ NaCl and protease inhibitor cocktail). Immunoprecipitation was performed with $5\mathrm{mg}$ rabbit anti-RUNX1 (Abcam, ab23980), rabbit anti-RUNX2 (CST, 8486S) coupled to Dynabeads Protein A overnight at $4^{\circ}C$ . Beads were washed, eluted, and reverse cross-linked. DNA was purified using the MinElute Reaction Clean up Kit (QIAGEN). The ChIP DNA library was constructed with VAHTS Turbo DNA Library Prep Kit for Illumina (Vazyme Biotech) according to the manufacturer's instructions. AMPure XP beads were used for purification steps. The library was quantified with VAHTS Library Quantification Kit for Illumina (Vazyme Biotech) and sequenced on an illumina Novaseq X PLUS.

For the CUT&Tag assay, 50,000 mature brown adipocytes or $1g$ inguinal white adipose tissue (iWAT) are used to isolate nuclei with the nuclei isolation kit. Next, nuclei were performed using the NovoNGS® CUT & Tag 2.0 A High-Sensitivity Kit (for Illumina®) (Cat# N259-YH01-01A, Novoprotein Scientific, Inc.) following the manufacturer's protocol. Primary antibodies including rabbit anti-HDAC1 (Abcam, ab7028), rabbit anti-HDAC2 (Abcam, ab32117), rabbit anti-RUNX1 (Abcam, ab23980), rabbit anti-RUNX2 (CST, 8486S), rabbit anti-EP300 (CST, 86377), and rabbit anti-H3K27AC (Abcam, ab4729). Secondary antibody (anti-rabbit IgG, Novoprotein, N269-01A). The library preparations were sequenced on Illumina Novaseq platform and 150 bp paired-end reads were generated. Raw sequencing reads were processed using fastp with parameters -q 20 --detect_adapter_for_pe --trim.poly_g to remove low-quality bases, adapter contamination, and poly-G tails. Cleaned reads were aligned to the mouse reference genome (mm39) using Bowtie2 (v2.2.5) $^{68}$ with default parameter. Properly

paired reads with high mapping quality (MAPQ ≥ 20) were retained using samtools view (-f 2 -q 20) ⁶⁸,⁶⁹ . PCR duplicates were removed using Picard Tools (https://broadinstitute.github.io/picard/). Replicates were merged using samtools merge for downstream analyses. Peak calling was performed with MACS2 (v2.1.1) using the parameters -q 0.05 -B --nomodel -f BAMPE --keep-dup all to identify genome-wide peaks $^{70}$ . BigWig files for visualization were generated with deepTools bamCoverage using the parameters --normalizeUsing BPM --effectiveGenomeSize 2728222451 -bs 10

(https://deeptools.readthedocs.io/en/develop/content/tools/bamCoverage.html). To search for enriched motifs within peaks, peak coordinates were converted from mm39 to mm10 using UCSC liftOver, and HOMER (findMotifsGenome.pl) was used with default parameter<sup>71</sup>. Genes with ChIP-seq peaks located within 20 kb upstream or 10 kb downstream of a gene body were considered putative target genes.

For high-quality peaks, MACS2 was run with parameters -q 0.05 --fe-cutoff 4 -B --nomodel -f BAMPE --keep-dup all. Reads falling within these peaks were quantified using Rsubread<sup>72</sup>. Peaks containing more than 50 reads were classified as high-quality peaks. Differential binding sites (DBS) were identified using the DiffBind R package (v3.8.4)<sup>73</sup> with a default FDR threshold of < 0.05 for samples with high-quality peaks.

# ATAC-seq and analyses

ATAC-seq was executed as previously described $^{74}$ . In brief, a total of 50,000 cells were washed with cold PBS twice, then centrifuged for 5 min at $300g$ at $4^{\circ}C$, then resuspended in $50~\mu L$ lysis buffer (10 mM Tris-HCl pH 7.4, 10 mM NaCl, 3 mM MgCl2, 0.2% (v/v) IGEPAL CA-630) and incubated at $4^{\circ}C$ for 15 min. The suspension of nuclei was centrifuged for 10 min at $300g$ at $4^{\circ}C$, followed by TruePrep TM DNA Library PrepKit V2 for Illumina® (Vazyme biotech, TD501-503). Then the libraries were purified by a Qiaquick PCR (QIAGEN) column. Finally, the ATAC library was sequenced on an illumina Novaseq X PLUS.

ATAC-seq raw reads were trimmed to remove low-quality reads and adapters using trim-galore (https://www.bioinformatics.babraham.ac.uk/projects/trim_galore/). Next, trimmed reads were aligned to the mouse reference genome (mm10) using Bowtie2 with the "--very-sensitive -X 1000" option.

SAMtools was applied to keep properly mapped reads and duplicated reads were removed by sambamba (version 0.8.2) $^{75}$ . The Counts Per Million mapped reads (CPM) normalization method of deepTools bamCoverage (version 3.5.1) was used to transform alignment bam files into read coverage files in bigWig format. Peak calling was performed by MACS2 with parameter "--SPMR --nomodel --shift -100 --extsize 200" analysis $^{76}$ . Differentially accessible peaks were identified using DiffBind R package (version 3.8.4) $^{73}$, peaks with threshold FDR of < 0.05 and fold change ≥ 2 were identified as significant differentially accessible regions, and stable regions were identified with a fold change $< 2$ . Motif enrichment analysis was performed using the HOMER function "findMotifsGenome.pl" with default options. Visualization was performed by Integrative Genomic Viewer genome (IGV) browser (v.2.8.0) using BigWig track files $^{77}$ .
